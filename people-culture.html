Section 01

Growing People Through Delegation

Delegation is not about offloading work. It is about deliberately creating opportunities for people to grow into new capabilities. The strongest teams are the ones where the leader is constantly working to make themselves less needed.

The Growth Ladder

Think of each person on your team as being at a specific level of readiness for any given type of work. Your job is to move people up this ladder — one rung at a time, with intention.

The 5 Levels of the Growth Ladder

  1. Observe — They watch you do it. You narrate your thinking out loud so they understand the "why" behind each decision.
  2. Assist — They help you with parts of it. You assign a specific slice while handling the rest, and debrief together afterward.
  3. Execute — They do it with your guidance. You set the direction, they do the work, and you review before it ships.
  4. Own — They do it independently. You are available for questions, but they drive the work end-to-end.
  5. Teach — They teach others how to do it. This is when you know the skill has truly transferred.

Your job is to move people up this ladder. Not all at once — one capability at a time, at a pace that stretches but does not overwhelm.

The 5 Cs of Delegation

Every time you delegate something, run through these five elements. Missing even one can turn a growth opportunity into a frustrating experience.

Context

Explain why this work matters. People make better decisions when they understand the bigger picture. "We need this because..." is the most important sentence you can say when delegating.

Clarity

Define what success looks like. Be specific about the outcome you expect, the quality bar, and any constraints. "You will know this is done when..." removes ambiguity.

Confidence

Tell them why you chose them for this. "I am asking you because I have seen you handle X well" is a simple sentence that unlocks motivation and signals trust.

Checkpoints

Agree on when you will check in. Not to micromanage, but to create natural moments for course correction. "Let us sync after you have the initial design" keeps everyone aligned.

Consequences

Be clear about what the results mean — for the project, the team, and their growth. Help them see how this piece of work connects to their development trajectory.

What to Delegate vs. What to Keep

Not everything should be delegated, and not everything should be kept. Here is a practical guide for thinking about the split.

Delegate (~80% of your time freed) Keep for yourself
Most MR reviews Cross-team architecture decisions
Sprint ceremonies facilitation Stakeholder management
Tech debt prioritization Scope decisions with business impact
Brown bag sessions and knowledge sharing Hiring and team composition
Design documents for known domains Technical direction and strategy
Debugging production issues Escalations and critical incidents

Stop Giving Answers

Framework: Building Decision-Making Muscle

When someone comes to you with a question, resist the urge to answer immediately. Instead, ask: "What options have you considered?"

This single question does three things at once: it validates that they have been thinking about the problem, it gives you insight into their reasoning process, and it builds their confidence in making decisions without you.

If they have not considered options yet, that is okay. Guide them: "Take 15 minutes, come back with two or three approaches and the trade-offs of each." Over time, they will start arriving with options already prepared — and eventually, they will not need to come to you at all.

💡

Normalizing imperfection: "Push what you have as a draft MR. Nobody expects the first attempt to be perfect." This one sentence, said consistently, removes one of the biggest blockers to delegation — the fear of not being good enough.

Section 02

Mentoring Done Right

Mentoring is one of the most rewarding parts of leadership — and one of the areas where a small shift in approach can make a dramatic difference in someone's growth trajectory.

Key Insight

Mentoring is one of the most impactful things a leader can do — and one of the most commonly misunderstood. Many well-intentioned actions that feel like mentoring are actually something else entirely.

Common Misconceptions

These are all valuable activities, but they are not mentoring. Recognizing the difference helps you be intentional about when you are doing each one.

"Helping with their current story"

Jumping in to unblock someone on their task is task support. It solves the immediate problem, but it does not build long-term capability. Task support is important — just be aware when you are doing it instead of mentoring.

"Giving technical direction"

Telling someone which approach to take is technical guidance. It gets the work done efficiently, but it can create dependency if overused. The goal is to help people develop their own judgment, not to be the source of every decision.

"Running status 1:1s"

Going through task lists and blockers is project management. It is necessary, but if that is all your 1:1s consist of, the growth conversations are not happening. Consider splitting your 1:1 time: half on current work, half on development.

"Giving direct answers"

Every direct answer you give is a learning opportunity that went unused. This does not mean never answer questions — sometimes urgency demands it. But when there is time, the question "What do you think?" is more powerful than any answer you could give.

What Effective Mentoring Looks Like

Effective mentoring is about helping people develop capabilities, judgment, and confidence — not just completing tasks.

Encouraging People to Do Hard Things

Stretch assignments, holding high standards, and expressing genuine belief in someone's ability to rise to a challenge. Growth happens at the edge of comfort, and a good mentor knows how to push someone there while making them feel supported.

Helping People See Their Gaps

The mirror function. Most people have blind spots about their own areas for growth. A great mentor reflects these back with empathy and specificity — not as criticism, but as an invitation to grow. "I have noticed X — would you like to work on that?"

Career Guidance

Connecting daily work to long-term growth. Help people see how the story they are working on today builds a skill they will need tomorrow. "This design doc exercise is not just about this feature — it is building the muscle you will need when you lead architecture decisions."

Being a Sounding Board

Help them think, not think for them. When someone brings you a problem, listen fully before responding. Ask clarifying questions. Reflect their reasoning back to them. Often, they already have the answer — they just need someone to help them see it.

The Prompting Questions Toolkit

Instead of telling, try asking. Here are questions organized by when to use them.

Framework: Prompting Questions by Stage

Before they start:

  • "What is your understanding of what needs to happen here?"
  • "What would you do first? Why?"
  • "What are the risks you see?"

When they are stuck:

  • "What have you tried so far?"
  • "What does your debugging tell you?"
  • "If you had to pick a direction right now, which way would you lean?"

When they propose a solution:

  • "What are the trade-offs of this approach?"
  • "What would you do differently if performance was the top priority? What about maintainability?"
  • "How would you explain this choice to someone who disagrees?"

After completion:

  • "What would you do differently next time?"
  • "What surprised you during this work?"
  • "What did you learn that you could teach someone else?"

Monthly Growth Check-In

Set aside 20 minutes once a month with each person for a dedicated growth conversation. This is separate from your regular 1:1 — it is focused entirely on their development.

Framework: Monthly Growth Check-In (5 Steps)

  1. Reflect — "What are you most proud of from the last month? What felt hard?"
  2. Identify gaps — "Where did you feel out of your depth? What skill, if you had it, would have made something easier?"
  3. Set a goal — Pick one specific, measurable growth goal for the next month. Not a task — a capability. Example: "Lead one design review independently."
  4. Career connect — "How does this goal connect to where you want to be in a year? Two years?"
  5. Support plan — "What do you need from me to make this happen? What resources, time, or air cover can I provide?"

Common Patterns

Pitfall: Answering Too Fast

When someone asks a question, giving the answer immediately. This feels efficient but robs them of the learning process. Over time, they learn to come to you first instead of reasoning through problems themselves.

Better: Setting Up Discovery

"That is a great question. Before I share my thinking, what approaches have you considered?" Then build on their ideas rather than replacing them. Even if their approach is not optimal, the act of reasoning through it builds lasting capability.

Pitfall: Only Mentoring on Tasks

Every conversation revolves around the current sprint, the current story, the current bug. The person gets better at executing but does not develop broader skills like communication, design thinking, or leadership.

Better: Mentoring the Whole Person

"I noticed you did a great job explaining the trade-offs in standup today. That is a skill that will serve you well as you grow. Want to try presenting the architecture overview at the next team sync?" Connect daily observations to bigger development themes.

💡

The true test: If you have been mentoring someone for 3 months and they still need the same level of guidance, it is time to change your approach. Either the pace is too fast, the method is not working, or there is a mismatch between the challenge and their readiness. Reflect honestly and adjust.

Section 03

Building a Feedback Culture

Feedback is the lifeblood of growth. Without it, people operate in the dark — guessing whether they are doing well, unsure of what to improve, and missing out on the recognition they deserve. Building a culture where feedback flows naturally is one of the highest-leverage things a leader can do.

What Healthy Feedback Looks Like

Frequent & Informal

Feedback should not be saved for formal reviews. The best feedback happens in the moment — a quick message after a good MR, a five-minute chat after a standup. When feedback is frequent, it stops feeling like a big event and starts feeling like normal communication.

Bidirectional

Feedback is not just top-down. Leaders need feedback too. Actively ask your team: "What is one thing I could do differently to support you better?" Model that receiving feedback is a strength, not a vulnerability.

Specific & Actionable

"Great job" is nice but not helpful. "The way you structured that MR with clear commit messages and a summary made it really easy to review — keep doing that" gives them something concrete to repeat.

Safe to Give and Receive

People will only give honest feedback if they believe it will not be held against them. This safety is built through consistent behavior over time — responding to feedback with gratitude, acting on it visibly, and never punishing honesty.

Radical Candor

Kim Scott's Radical Candor framework is one of the most practical tools for understanding feedback dynamics. It maps two dimensions — how much you care personally and how directly you challenge — into four quadrants.

Challenge Directly Don't Challenge
Care Personally Radical Candor — The goal. You care enough to tell the truth, and you do it with empathy. "I am telling you this because I want you to succeed, and I think this is getting in your way." Ruinous Empathy — You care, but you avoid saying the hard thing. The person never gets the feedback they need to grow. This feels kind but is actually harmful.
Don't Care Personally Obnoxious Aggression — You challenge, but without caring. The feedback may be accurate but lands as an attack. People shut down instead of growing. Manipulative Insincerity — You neither care nor challenge. Passive-aggressive behavior, talking behind backs, or giving insincere praise to avoid conflict.
⚠️

Where most leaders default: Ruinous Empathy. We care about our people and do not want to hurt them, so we soften feedback until it loses its meaning — or avoid giving it altogether. The result is that people never hear what they need to hear to grow.

The SBI Feedback Model

When you need to give specific feedback — whether positive or constructive — the SBI model keeps it clear and grounded in facts rather than opinions.

Framework: Situation → Behavior → Impact

Situation: When and where did it happen? ("In yesterday's sprint review...")

Behavior: What specifically did they do? Observable actions, not interpretations. ("...you walked the stakeholders through the trade-offs before presenting your recommendation...")

Impact: What was the result? ("...and it helped them understand why we chose this approach. They left with confidence in the direction.")

Positive example: "In yesterday's sprint review, you walked the stakeholders through the trade-offs before presenting your recommendation, and it helped them understand why we chose this approach. That is exactly the kind of communication we need more of."

Constructive example: "In yesterday's MR, the test coverage for the new endpoint was below our team standard. When tests are missing, other team members have to add them later or we ship with less confidence. Can we talk about what got in the way and how to address it?"

Building Feedback Into Your Routine

Feedback should not require a special occasion. Here are natural moments where feedback fits naturally into existing workflows.

Framework: Feedback Touchpoints

  • After MR reviews — A quick message: "Really clean implementation" or "I noticed X — here is why it matters."
  • After sprints — In retro or privately: "Here is what I saw go well this sprint, and here is one thing to focus on next sprint."
  • In monthly 1:1s — Dedicated time for deeper feedback conversations, connected to their growth goals.
  • In retros (shoutout section) — Create a standing "shoutouts" slot in your retrospectives where team members can publicly recognize each other.
  • In the moment — The most powerful feedback is timely. If you see something worth commenting on, do it now. A quick Slack message or a 2-minute conversation has more impact than a note saved for next month.

Leader's Growth Check

Run through this checklist monthly for each person on your team. If you find yourself answering "no" to several of these, that is a signal to invest more attention.

Monthly Per-Person Feedback Check

👁

The invisible person check: In every team, there is someone who is quiet, competent enough not to cause problems, and easy to overlook. They rarely ask for help, they deliver steadily, and because they do not create noise, they do not get attention. Make sure they get explicit, dedicated feedback and growth conversations. They deserve it just as much as anyone else.

Recommended Reading

Radical Candor

By Kim Scott. The definitive guide to caring personally while challenging directly. Full of practical advice for having the conversations that matter most.

Thanks for the Feedback

By Douglas Stone & Sheila Heen. Focuses on the receiving side of feedback — understanding why feedback is hard to hear and how to get better at taking it in. Essential for leaders who want to model receptiveness.

Section 04

Building Ownership

Ownership is not a personality trait — it is a culture. It is built through norms, habits, and the consistent message that "this is ours, and we all take care of it." When ownership is strong, problems get solved before they escalate, knowledge flows freely, and the team operates with genuine autonomy.

What Ownership Looks Like

It's Everyone's Code

See a bug? Fix it or raise it — even if you did not write that code. Someone asks a question on a channel? If you know the answer, respond. Ownership means the whole codebase is your responsibility, not just the parts you authored.

Finish What You Start

If you pick up a task, see it through to completion. If something changes and you cannot finish it, hand it off explicitly — with context, not just a status update. Ownership means the work is done, not just started.

Hold Each Other Accountable

Accountability should be peer-to-peer, not just top-down. When a teammate misses something, the team addresses it directly and supportively. This only works when the team trusts each other enough to have those conversations.

Proactively Pick Up Work

Miscellaneous work that appears on channels — a flaky test, a question from another team, a small config change — is not "someone else's job." In a team with strong ownership, people pick these up without being asked.

3 Ownership-Building Habits

Ownership does not emerge from speeches. It comes from repeated small behaviors that become team norms.

Framework: 3 Habits That Build Ownership

  1. Rotate domain ownership monthly — Assign each person a domain area (authentication, payments, CI pipeline, etc.) that they are the "go-to" for that month. Rotate regularly so knowledge spreads and no one becomes a permanent bottleneck.
  2. The 2-hour rule — Two sides of the same coin: if you are stuck for more than 2 hours, ask for help. If someone asks you a question, respond within 2 hours. This creates a fast feedback loop and prevents both silent struggling and unresponsive knowledge silos.
  3. Continuous codebase improvement — For practices like the Boy Scout Rule and tech debt ownership, see Tech Debt as Culture. Embed these into your team norms so they become automatic, not occasional.
⚠️

Watch for knowledge silos: When context is concentrated in a few people and others do not respond to questions, bottlenecks form. This is a culture problem that leaders must address directly — with norms, not blame. Set the expectation that responding to team questions is part of everyone's job, rotate ownership to spread knowledge, and recognize people who actively share context.

Running Inclusive Dev Huddles

A dev huddle is only as valuable as the diversity of perspectives it surfaces. If the same two or three people dominate every discussion, you are not getting the full picture — and the quieter members are not getting the growth opportunity of contributing their thinking.

Why People Stay Silent

Before you can fix participation, you need to understand why people hold back. It is rarely because they have nothing to say.

They Don't Feel Safe

They worry about saying something wrong, being judged, or looking inexperienced. This is especially true for newer team members or those from cultures where challenging seniors is uncommon.

They Don't Have Context

They were not included in earlier discussions, have not seen the relevant documents, or joined the project recently. Without context, even smart people stay quiet because they feel they cannot add value.

The Format Doesn't Invite Them

Open-floor discussions naturally favor extroverts and fast thinkers. If your format is "whoever speaks first wins," you are structurally excluding people who need a moment to organize their thoughts.

Write First, Talk Second

Framework: Silent Writing Before Discussion

Before opening the floor for discussion on any topic:

  1. Share the question or topic on screen.
  2. Give everyone 3 minutes of silent writing to note down their thoughts.
  3. Then go around and have each person share what they wrote.

This simple technique removes real-time pressure, gives introverts equal footing, and produces higher-quality input because everyone has time to think before speaking. It also prevents anchoring — the first person to speak does not set the direction for everyone else.

The Round Robin Rule

Framework: Structured Turn-Taking

Go around the room so that every person has a turn to speak. Two important guidelines:

  • Experienced members speak last. When senior people speak first, others often defer to their opinion instead of sharing their own. Reversing the order gives junior members the space to form and express their own views.
  • "Pass" is always allowed. No one should be forced to speak. But by creating the expectation that everyone gets a turn, you normalize participation and make it easier for quieter people to join in when they are ready.

Facilitator, Not Dominator

Framework: The Facilitator Mindset

  • Ask questions, do not provide answers. Your role in a huddle is to draw out the team's thinking, not to present your own. Save your opinions for last, or share them outside the huddle if the team reaches a good conclusion on their own.
  • Redirect when someone dominates. "That is a great point. Let us hear from others — anyone have a different perspective?" This is not rude; it is inclusive facilitation.
  • Never shut down ideas publicly. Even if an idea is not viable, acknowledge the thinking behind it before redirecting: "I can see why you would approach it that way. What if we also considered..."

Specific Tactics

Assign topics in advance ▼

Share the agenda and discussion topics at least a day before the huddle. This gives people time to think, research, and prepare their thoughts — especially valuable for those who process information best when they have time to reflect.

Rotate facilitation ▼

Do not always facilitate the huddle yourself. Rotate the role among team members. Facilitation is a leadership skill, and giving people the chance to practice it builds their confidence and changes the dynamic of the conversation.

Ask "What questions do you have?" not "Any questions?" ▼

"Any questions?" implies the default is to have none. "What questions do you have?" assumes questions exist and invites them. This subtle language shift measurably increases participation. Try it and notice the difference.

Celebrate wrong answers ▼

When someone suggests an approach that will not work, respond with genuine appreciation: "I am glad you raised that — it is a natural thing to consider, and here is what makes this situation different." When wrong answers are treated as valuable contributions to the discussion, people stop being afraid to speak up.

The "Plus One" follow-up technique ▼

After someone shares a point, ask: "Who can build on that?" or "Does anyone have a +1 or a different angle?" This technique validates the original speaker, invites others to contribute, and creates a collaborative rather than competitive dynamic.

💡

The leader's paradox: The more you talk in a huddle, the less others will. If you are speaking more than 30% of the time, you are dominating, not leading. Track it for a week — you might be surprised by the ratio. The best huddles are the ones where the leader barely speaks.

Section 06

Having Honest Conversations

The conversations you avoid are the ones that matter most. Learning to have them — with clarity, empathy, and genuine care — is one of the most important leadership skills you can develop.

Key Insight

The most caring thing you can do for someone's growth is to be honest with them — delivered with empathy and specificity. Avoiding a hard conversation is not kindness; it is withholding the information someone needs to improve. The discomfort of the conversation is temporary. The cost of silence is permanent.

The Conversation Formula

Framework: Observation + Impact + Question + Support

This four-part structure helps you prepare for honest conversations. It keeps the focus on growth rather than blame.

  1. Observation — What specifically did you observe? Stick to facts, not interpretations. "I noticed that the last three MRs did not include tests."
  2. Impact — What is the effect? On the team, the codebase, or their growth. "This means other team members are adding tests after the fact, which slows the team down and creates frustration."
  3. Question — Ask, do not assume. There may be context you are missing. "Can you help me understand what is happening? Is there something getting in the way?"
  4. Support — Offer to help. "I want to help you get past this. Would it help if we paired on the next one to establish a testing pattern together?"

Full example: "Hey, I wanted to chat about something I have noticed. In the last three MRs, the test coverage was below our team standard. I know writing tests can feel like it slows things down, but when they are missing, other people have to add them later, and it creates a ripple effect on the team's velocity. I am not saying this to criticize — I want to understand what is going on. Is something making it harder to include tests? And how can I help?"

Scripts for Common Situations

These are not word-for-word scripts you must follow — they are starting points that show the tone and approach. Adapt them to your own voice and the specific situation. All of these conversations should happen in a private 1:1 setting, never in front of others.

"Tests are consistently missing from MRs" ▼

Setting: Private 1:1

"I wanted to talk about something I have been noticing in your recent MRs. The test coverage has been below what we aim for as a team. I know you are capable of writing good tests — I have seen you do it before. I am curious what is happening. Is there a time pressure issue? Is the testing setup for this service making it harder? I want to figure out what is in the way and help you address it, because consistent test coverage is something the team relies on."

"Not responding to team questions on channels" ▼

Setting: Private 1:1

"I have noticed that when questions come up on the team channel — especially about areas you have context on — they sometimes go unanswered. I know you are busy, and I am not asking you to drop everything. But when people do not get responses, they either stay blocked or have to escalate, and it creates a bottleneck. Could we talk about a way to make this work? Maybe checking the channel at a couple of set times each day? What would work for you?"

"Started something but didn't finish" ▼

Setting: Private 1:1

"I wanted to check in about the monitoring dashboard work. It was picked up a couple of sprints ago but seems to have stalled. I am not trying to assign blame — I know priorities shift. But when work is started and not finished, it creates uncertainty for the rest of the team. Can we talk about what happened and figure out a path forward? If the priority has changed, let us make that explicit. If something else got in the way, let us address that."

"Executing well but not leading" ▼

Setting: Private 1:1

"You are doing great work on the execution side — your code quality is strong and you deliver consistently. I want to talk about the next level for you, which is about influence beyond your own tasks. Things like: proactively improving team processes, mentoring others, or taking on a bigger design challenge. I see the potential in you for this, and I would love to help you grow into it. What sounds interesting to you? What feels like a stretch you would be excited about?"

"Code quality doesn't match experience level" ▼

Setting: Private 1:1

"I want to have an honest conversation because I think it will help. Looking at your recent MRs, the code quality is not quite where I would expect given your experience. I am seeing things like missing edge case handling, large methods that could be broken down, and inconsistent naming. I am not saying this to be harsh — I am saying it because I believe you can do better, and I want to help you get there. Can we look at a recent MR together and walk through some of the patterns I am talking about? I would also like to understand if there is something contributing to this — time pressure, unfamiliar domain, something else?"

⏰

The 24-hour rule: Have the conversation within 24 hours of observing the behavior. The longer you wait, the harder it gets, the less specific your memory becomes, and the more likely the pattern will repeat. Timeliness is a form of respect — it shows you care enough to address things promptly.

Recommended Reading

Crucial Conversations

By Kerry Patterson, Joseph Grenny, Ron McMillan, and Al Switzler. The foundational guide to handling high-stakes conversations where opinions differ and emotions run strong. Provides practical tools for staying in dialogue when it matters most.

Section 07

Hiring & Team Composition

Who you bring onto the team shapes everything that follows — the quality of work, the culture, the pace of growth. Getting hiring right is one of the most important investments a leader can make.

Key Insight

The highest-leverage decision a leader makes is who joins the team. A great hire raises everyone's game. A poor hire creates months of drag. Invest the time upfront.

What to Evaluate Beyond Technical Skills

Technical ability is the baseline. What separates good hires from great ones is what they bring beyond code.

Learning Orientation

Do they seek feedback? Do they learn from mistakes? Are they curious?

Why it matters: Technical skills can be taught; the drive to learn cannot be.

Ownership Mindset

Do they take initiative? Do they follow through? Do they see problems as theirs to solve?

Why it matters: Teams with ownership do not need managers — they need leaders who set direction.

Communication Clarity

Can they explain complex ideas simply? Do they ask good questions?

Why it matters: Engineers who cannot communicate create bottlenecks and misunderstandings.

Team Amplification

Do they make others better? Do they share knowledge? Do they help when not asked?

Why it matters: A 10x engineer who hoards knowledge is worth less than an 8x engineer who lifts everyone.

Onboarding That Actually Works

A great hire can still fail if the onboarding is poor. Structure the first three months deliberately.

Framework: The First 90 Days

  1. Before Day 1 — Local setup works, accounts created, buddy assigned. Nobody should spend their first day fighting tooling.
  2. Week 1 — Pair on a real (small) task. Not just reading docs. Getting into the code immediately builds confidence and context faster than any onboarding document.
  3. Week 2–4 — Gradually increase scope. Explicit check-ins on what is confusing. Ask: "What surprised you? What does not make sense yet?"
  4. Month 2 — First independent feature. Review with extra care and encouragement. This is where they start building ownership.
  5. Month 3 — They should be contributing at baseline. If not, investigate what is blocking. Is it unclear expectations, missing context, or a deeper issue?
💡

The interview question that reveals the most: "Tell me about a time you made something significantly better that was not your job." Ownership shows up in stories, not claims.

Section 08

Frequent Feedback & Progress Tracking

Feedback is not a quarterly event — it is a daily practice. The leaders who grow their people fastest are the ones who build feedback into the rhythm of normal work, not the ones who save it for formal reviews.

Key Insight

Feedback given months later is almost worthless. The most effective feedback is given within hours — while the context is fresh and the person can immediately apply it. Great leaders build feedback into daily work, not quarterly reviews.

The Feedback Rhythm

Feedback is not a single event. It is a continuous practice with different intensities at different cadences.

Framework: The Feedback Rhythm

  • Daily — Quick acknowledgments in MR reviews, Slack ("Great catch on that edge case"), standups. These micro-moments of recognition add up.
  • Weekly — One specific piece of feedback per team member (positive or constructive). Takes 2 minutes. The consistency matters more than the depth.
  • Bi-weekly — Brief 1:1 check-ins focused on growth, not status. Ask: "What is going well? What is frustrating? What do you need from me?"
  • Monthly — The Growth Check-In (deeper). Review goals set last month. Identify gaps. Set next goals. Connect to career path.
  • Quarterly — Formal assessment aligned with expectations set at cycle start. No surprises — everything here was discussed in the weeklies and monthlies.

Key Insight

If your quarterly review contains surprises for the person being reviewed, your feedback rhythm is broken. They should already know exactly where they stand.

Tracking Progress Without Micromanaging

There is a meaningful difference between staying informed and hovering. Great leaders track closely without breathing down necks.

Framework: Tracking vs. Micromanaging

Micromanaging

"I ask for hourly updates. I review every line before it merges. I tell people how to solve problems."

Tracking

"I check the board daily. I read MRs. I notice patterns. I bring up concerns in 1:1s."

5 Practical Tracking Habits

Daily/Weekly Tracking Practices

The Catch-Up Cadence

Not everyone needs the same frequency of attention. Adjust your cadence based on where each person is.

Framework: Different Rhythms for Different Needs

  • People who are ramping up or struggling — Weekly 30-minute 1:1s. They need more guidance, more encouragement, and faster feedback loops.
  • People who are performing well — Bi-weekly 1:1s. They are on track but still benefit from regular check-ins on growth and direction.
  • People who are strong and independent — Monthly 1:1s. But never skip entirely — even strong performers need attention, recognition, and a space to talk about what is next for them.

The rule: If you have not had a meaningful conversation with someone in 2 weeks, you are losing signal on their growth and engagement.

💡

The most dangerous state is when someone stops giving you problems. It usually means they have stopped caring, not that everything is fine. Check in.

Section 09

Navigating Underperformance

Addressing underperformance is one of the hardest parts of leadership. It requires courage, empathy, and structure. Done well, it can turn someone's trajectory around. Done poorly — or not at all — it erodes the entire team.

Key Insight

Most leaders avoid addressing underperformance until it is too late. By then, the team has compensated silently, high performers are frustrated, and the underperformer is confused because nobody told them. Early, honest intervention is kinder to everyone.

The Escalation Path

Underperformance does not jump from "everything is fine" to "we need to let you go." There is a clear progression, and each step gives the person a genuine opportunity to improve.

Framework: The Escalation Path

  1. Informal feedback — Immediate, specific, within 24 hours of observing the issue. "Hey, I noticed X in your MR today. Here is why it matters and what I would suggest instead." Most issues resolve here when addressed early.
  2. Pattern conversation — When you see the same issue 3+ times: "I have noticed a pattern. In the last month, X happened in 3 situations. What is going on?" This shifts from incident to trend.
  3. Formal expectations reset — Written agreement on specific improvements with a timeline. "In the next 4 weeks, I need to see X, Y, Z. Let us check in weekly." Clear, documented, unambiguous.
  4. Performance improvement plan — If the expectations reset does not work, formal documentation with HR/management. Clear milestones, clear timeline, clear consequences.
  5. Separation — If the person cannot meet the bar despite support, the kindest thing for everyone is to help them find a better fit. Prolonging a failing situation helps no one.

Diagnosing Underperformance

Before escalating, invest the time to understand the root cause. The intervention depends entirely on the diagnosis.

Skill Gap?

They want to perform but lack the capability. Invest in training, pairing, and mentoring. This is the most solvable type of underperformance.

Motivation Gap?

They have the skill but not the drive. Understand what has changed. Boredom? Burnout? Personal issues? Wrong role? The conversation here is exploratory, not corrective.

Clarity Gap?

Were expectations actually clear? Sometimes the leader failed to communicate, not the person failed to perform. Before blaming execution, audit the briefing.

Fit Gap?

Sometimes good people are in the wrong role. Help them find the right one. A struggling backend engineer might thrive in DevOps, or vice versa.

Protecting the Team While Supporting the Individual

When someone is underperforming, the rest of the team feels it. Balancing support for the individual with fairness to the team requires deliberate action.

Framework: Balancing Team and Individual Needs

What Not to Do

Do not publicly shame or call out underperformers. Do not let the team compensate silently for months — that breeds resentment and burns out your best people.

What to Do Instead

Redistribute work transparently when someone is on a growth plan. Check in with high performers: "I know you have been carrying extra load. I am addressing it. Thank you for your patience."

💡

Having the first conversation early is hard. Having it 6 months late is much harder — for you, for them, and for the team. The sooner you name it, the sooner you can fix it.

Section 10

Running Effective 1:1s

The 1:1 is your most powerful leadership tool. It is where trust is built, feedback is exchanged, problems surface early, and growth happens. Yet most leaders run 1:1s as status updates — a waste of the most valuable 30 minutes on your calendar.

Key Insight

If your 1:1s consist entirely of going through a task list, you are doing project management, not leadership. The sprint board already tracks tasks. The 1:1 exists for the things the board cannot capture: how someone is really doing, what is frustrating them, where they want to grow, and what feedback they need to hear.

The 1:1 Rhythm

Not everyone needs the same cadence. Match the frequency to where each person is right now.

Framework: The 1:1 Rhythm

  • Weekly (30 min) — For people who are new, ramping up, or struggling. They need more guidance, faster feedback loops, and frequent reassurance. Do not skip these.
  • Fortnightly (30–45 min) — For people who are performing well and operating with reasonable independence. This is the default cadence for most of your team. Enough to stay connected without being overbearing.
  • Monthly (45–60 min) — For strong, senior performers who need less frequent check-ins. But never skip entirely — even independent people need recognition, direction, and a space to talk about what is next.

The rule: If you have not had a meaningful conversation with someone in 2 weeks, you are losing signal. Adjust the cadence, not the existence.

The 3-Part 1:1 Structure

A simple structure that turns every 1:1 into a growth conversation, not a status report.

Framework: The 3-Part 1:1 Structure

  1. Part 1: Their agenda (15 min) — Start by asking: "What is on your mind?" This is their time. Let them set the direction. If they say "nothing," try: "What was the hardest part of your week?" or "What would make next week better than this one?" Resist filling the silence — give them space to think.
  2. Part 2: Your feedback and observations (10 min) — Share one specific piece of feedback — positive or constructive. Use the SBI model. Also share any context they need: upcoming changes, stakeholder feedback, team dynamics they should know about. This is where you add value they cannot get anywhere else.
  3. Part 3: Growth and forward-looking (5 min) — Connect to their development goals. "How is the [growth goal] going?" "Is there a stretch opportunity coming up that excites you?" End on a forward note. This is how 1:1s become growth conversations, not just check-ins.

Questions That Unlock Real Conversations

Beyond Status

  • "What is the most frustrating thing about your work right now?"
  • "What is one thing I could do differently to support you better?"
  • "Is there anything you have been wanting to say but have not found the right moment?"
  • "What would you do differently if you were in my position?"
  • "What is draining your energy? What is giving you energy?"

Growth-Focused

  • "Where do you feel most stretched right now?"
  • "What skill do you wish you were better at?"
  • "What kind of work do you want to be doing in a year that you are not doing now?"
  • "Is there someone on the team or in the company you would like to learn from?"
  • "What would make you feel more confident in [specific area]?"

Common Patterns

Pitfall: The Status Update 1:1

Walking through the task board, asking "how is the feature going," and ending when the list is done. The person leaves feeling managed, not led. Nothing personal was discussed, no feedback was exchanged, and no growth happened.

Better: The Growth-Oriented 1:1

Their agenda first, specific feedback, and a forward-looking question. The person leaves feeling heard, knowing where they stand, and having something to work toward. Even a 15-minute 1:1 in this format beats a 60-minute status review.

1:1 Anti-Patterns to Avoid

Framework: 1:1 Anti-Patterns to Avoid

  • Cancelling regularly — Every cancellation signals "you are not a priority." If you must cancel, reschedule the same day. Never let more than 2 weeks pass without one.
  • Doing all the talking — If you speak more than 40% of the time, you are monologuing, not connecting. Ask a question and wait.
  • Only discussing problems — If every 1:1 is about what is going wrong, people will start dreading them. Celebrate wins. Acknowledge effort. Make it a space people look forward to.
  • Skipping the quiet performers — The people who cause no problems are often the ones getting the least attention. They need 1:1s just as much — for recognition, growth, and preventing the disengagement that comes from being invisible.
  • No notes or follow-through — If you commit to something in a 1:1 and forget, trust erodes. Keep brief notes. Follow up on action items. "Last time you mentioned X — how did that go?" shows you were listening.
💡

Start every 1:1 with "What is on your mind?" and resist the urge to fill silence. The best insights come after the first pause, not before it.

Scripts for Different 1:1 Moments

"When someone says everything is fine (but it is not)" ▼

Setting: 1:1, when you sense something is off but they deflect.

"I hear you saying things are fine, and I trust your read. But I have noticed [specific observation — quieter in meetings, shorter commit messages, skipped the social call]. I am not reading into it — I just want to check in. Is there anything I am not seeing? You do not have to share if it is personal, but I want you to know the door is open."

"Opening a growth conversation" ▼

Setting: Monthly 1:1, dedicated to development.

"I want to use today's time to talk about your growth, not the sprint. Looking back at the last month, what are you most proud of? And if you could level up one skill that would make the biggest difference in your work, what would it be? Let us figure out how to create an opportunity for that."

"Giving constructive feedback in a 1:1" ▼

Setting: Regular 1:1, after observing a pattern.

"There is something I want to share because I think it will help you. In the last two sprint reviews, when [stakeholder] asked about timelines, the answer was quite technical and detailed. I could see them losing the thread. For someone in their position, they need the headline first — 'we are on track, with one risk' — and detail only if they ask. Would it help if we dry-ran the next update together? I think one practice round would make a big difference."
