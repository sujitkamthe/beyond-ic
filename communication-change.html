Section 1

Communicate with Structure and Presence

Technical skill earns you a seat at the table. Communication skill determines whether anyone listens once you sit down. The frameworks below are not about polish or performance — they are about making your ideas land so you can have real influence.

The Pyramid Principle

Created by Barbara Minto during her time at McKinsey, the Pyramid Principle is deceptively simple: lead with your conclusion, then support it with grouped arguments and evidence. It is the single highest-leverage communication skill you can develop as a leader.

Why Engineers Default to Bottom-Up

Engineers are trained to show their work. In school, you got partial credit for showing your reasoning. In code reviews, you walk through the logic step by step. This habit carries into how you communicate: you start with what you investigated, what you found, and eventually arrive at a conclusion.

The problem is that executives and stakeholders are optimising for speed. They make dozens of decisions a day and need the answer first to decide whether the supporting detail is worth their time. When you build to a conclusion, they are mentally shouting "get to the point" while trying to look patient.

The Structure

  1. State your conclusion first. One clear sentence. Your audience's attention is highest right now — use it.
  2. Group your supporting arguments into 2–4 logical categories. Each group should stand on its own as a reason to believe the conclusion.
  3. Back each argument with evidence. Data, examples, or reasoning that makes each point credible.
Bottom-Up (common engineer default)

"We've been investigating the checkout latency issue. First, we profiled the API gateway and noticed some overhead in the auth middleware. Then we looked at the database queries and found three N+1 queries in the order history endpoint. We also checked the Redis cache and it turns out the TTL was set to 5 seconds instead of 5 minutes due to a config typo. So we think we can get checkout latency from 3.2 seconds down to under 800 milliseconds with about 4 days of work."

Pyramid Style (lead with the answer)

"We can cut checkout latency by 75% — from 3.2 seconds to under 800ms — with 4 days of work. Three issues are compounding: N+1 database queries in order history, a misconfigured cache TTL, and unnecessary auth middleware overhead. Here is the breakdown and proposed fix for each..."

💡

A good test: Before sending a message or starting a presentation, write your key point in a single sentence. If you cannot do it, you are not ready to communicate yet — you are still thinking. That is fine, but know the difference.

MECE Thinking

MECE stands for Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. It is a way of breaking down a problem so that your categories do not overlap and nothing falls through the cracks. It matters because sloppy categorisation leads to double-counting, missed root causes, and analysis that feels thorough but isn't.

The Two Rules

  • Mutually Exclusive: Every item belongs in exactly one category. If something could fit in two buckets, your buckets are wrong.
  • Collectively Exhaustive: Your categories, taken together, cover the entire problem. If something could exist outside all your buckets, you have a gap.

Worked Example

Your manager asks: "Why are we missing sprint deadlines?" You could brainstorm a list of reasons, but that tends to produce scattered, overlapping ideas. A MECE breakdown forces rigour:

  1. Estimation issues — stories are consistently larger than estimated, complexity is underestimated, spikes are not timeboxed
  2. Scope issues — requirements change mid-sprint, stakeholders add work after planning, "small" requests get accepted without adjusting commitments
  3. Execution issues — developers are blocked waiting for reviews, context switching between projects, skill gaps on specific technologies
  4. Process issues — acceptance criteria are unclear, QA handoff is slow, deployment pipeline is unreliable

These four categories do not overlap (a problem fits in one and only one) and together they cover every possible cause. You can now investigate each systematically and present findings clearly, rather than chasing anecdotes.

Issue → Impact → Action

Most people are good at describing problems. Far fewer are good at explaining why the problem matters and what they need. This three-part formula gives your listener everything they need to respond effectively.

The Formula

  1. Issue: What is happening? (one or two factual sentences)
  2. Impact: Why does it matter? Who is affected, and how badly? (translate to business terms when possible)
  3. Action: What do you need? What are you already doing? (be specific — "I need a decision by Thursday" is better than "thoughts?")

Why People Fail at This

Most people stop at the Issue. They describe the problem in detail — what is broken, when it started, what they have tried. The listener is left wondering: "How bad is this? Do I need to do something? What are they asking me for?" Issue-Impact-Action removes that ambiguity entirely.

Example

Issue: "Our CI pipeline has been flaky for the past two weeks — about 15% of builds fail on tests that pass locally."

Impact: "Developers are re-running pipelines 2–3 times per MR, adding roughly 30 minutes of wait time per merge. Across the team, that is about 6 hours of lost developer time per week."

Action: "I want to allocate 3 days next sprint to investigate and fix the flaky tests. I need your support to protect that capacity from feature work."

Situation → Complication → Resolution

SCR is a storytelling framework that works because it mirrors how humans naturally process information. We need context first (situation), then tension (complication), then relief (resolution). It is especially powerful for proposals, escalations, and persuading decision-makers.

The Three Parts

  1. Situation: What is the current state? This should be uncontroversial — something everyone would agree on.
  2. Complication: What has changed, or what is the tension? This creates the "so what" that demands a response.
  3. Resolution: What do you recommend? This is your proposed path forward.

Example: Proposing a Tech Investment

Situation: "We deploy to production twice a week. Each deploy requires a manual regression cycle that takes two testers a full day."

Complication: "The product team wants to move to daily deploys next quarter to support faster experimentation. At our current pace, that would require doubling our QA capacity — which is not in the budget."

Resolution: "I propose we invest 3 sprints in end-to-end test automation for the critical paths. This would cut regression from 8 hours to 20 minutes and make daily deploys feasible without additional headcount. The investment pays for itself within 6 weeks."

Executive Presence

Executive presence is not a personality trait you either have or don't. It is a collection of behaviours that you can practise and improve over time. It boils down to this: when the stakes are high, can you communicate with clarity, confidence, and composure?

Speaking in Leadership Forums

  • Prepare your top 3 points before the meeting. Even 5 minutes of preparation dramatically changes how you show up.
  • Lead with the headline. "Delivery is on track, with one risk I want to flag." Then provide detail only if asked.
  • Be concise. In senior forums, brevity signals confidence. Aim for 30 seconds. Elaborate only when invited.
  • Use numbers. "We reduced incident response time from 45 minutes to 12" is far more compelling than "we got better at incidents."
  • Say "I don't know, but I'll find out by EOD" when you genuinely do not know. Guessing erodes trust faster than any knowledge gap.

Handling Tough Questions

  • Don't rush. A 2-second pause before answering signals thoughtfulness, not uncertainty. Take the breath.
  • Reframe if needed. "That is a good question. The way I would frame it is..." lets you steer toward solid ground.
  • Acknowledge gaps honestly. "I want to verify those numbers before I commit. Can I follow up by tomorrow?"
  • Bridge to what you know. "I am not sure about X specifically, but what I can tell you is Y, which speaks to the same concern."
  • Always follow up. If you said you would get back to someone, do it. Reliability compounds into credibility over time.

Demo Confidence

  • Know your audience. Executives care about outcomes and risks. Other engineers want technical depth. Adjust your level of detail.
  • Tell a story. "Before: users filled out a 12-field form. After: one click. Here is the impact on completion rates." Impact over implementation.
  • Prepare for failure. Demos break. Have screenshots or a recording as backup. "Let me walk you through the flow while we sort this out" beats freezing.
  • End with what is next. "Next sprint we are adding X. The risk we are watching is Y." This shows you are thinking ahead, not just presenting what is done.

Building Confidence Over Time

  • Start with written updates. If live communication feels daunting, begin with concise written summaries. Build the muscle in a lower-stakes format.
  • Dry-run with a peer. Before a big presentation, spend 5 minutes rehearsing with someone you trust. "Does this flow? Am I missing anything?"
  • Observe leaders you admire. Watch how they structure updates, handle pushback, and recover from mistakes. This is a skill, not a personality trait.
  • Volunteer for small opportunities. Present in a team demo before presenting to leadership. Each rep builds capacity for the next one.
📚

Go deeper: Read The Pyramid Principle by Barbara Minto for the definitive guide to structured communication. For presence, Executive Presence by Sylvia Ann Hewlett breaks the concept into concrete, practicable skills.

Managing Up

Your relationship with your manager is one of the most consequential factors in your team's success. Managing up is not about politics — it is about building a partnership where information flows well in both directions.

Key Insight

Managing up is not political maneuvering — it is making sure your manager has what they need to support you and your team. When you manage up well, your team gets more resources, more air cover, and better decisions. When you don't, your manager is blindsided, your team's work is invisible, and good ideas die in silence.

What Your Manager Needs From You

Most managers will not tell you this directly, but there are four things they rely on from every leader who reports to them:

No Surprises

Surface risks early — not at the deadline. Your manager would rather hear "this might slip" on Tuesday than "this slipped" on Friday. Early warning gives them time to help, adjust expectations, or re-prioritise. Late surprises make both of you look bad and erode trust.

The rule: If there is a 30% chance something will go wrong, mention it now. Don't wait until it becomes a certainty.

Context Without Detail

Your manager needs to understand impact, not implementation. They do not need to know which database index you are adding — they need to know that checkout performance will improve by 40% and it will take 3 days. Save the technical detail for when they ask, or for written documentation.

The test: Can you explain the situation in 3 sentences that a non-technical VP would understand?

Recommendations, Not Problems

Bring options with your preferred choice and reasoning. "We have a problem" puts the burden on your manager. "We have a problem, here are three options, I recommend B because..." makes you a partner in solving it. Even if they choose differently, you have shown leadership thinking.

The formula: "Here is the situation. I see three paths. I recommend X because Y. What do you think?"

Predictability

Consistent delivery and a consistent communication rhythm. Your manager should never have to chase you for an update. Establish a cadence — whether it is a weekly email, a 1:1 agenda, or a Slack summary — and stick to it. Predictability builds trust faster than any single heroic effort.

The habit: Send a brief weekly update every Friday: what shipped, what is in progress, what risks are on the horizon.

When Your Manager Disagrees with Your Technical Direction

This happens to every leader eventually. The way you handle it defines the relationship. Here is a framework that respects both your expertise and their authority:

A Step-by-Step Approach

  1. Understand their concern first. Before defending your position, ask: "What are you worried about?" Often their objection is about risk, timeline, or stakeholder perception — not the technical approach itself. Understanding the real concern changes the conversation entirely.
  2. Present your reasoning with data, not authority. "I ran load tests comparing both approaches, and option A handles 3x the throughput at the same cost" is persuasive. "Trust me, I have done this before" is not. Data invites dialogue; authority invites defensiveness.
  3. Offer an experiment. "Can we try my approach for 2 weeks and measure the results? If the metrics don't improve, we switch to your preferred approach." This lowers the stakes and lets evidence settle the disagreement.
  4. If overruled, commit and execute. Disagree and commit is not just a team principle — it applies to your relationship with your manager too. Execute their decision wholeheartedly. If the results confirm your concern, you will have earned the credibility to revisit the decision with evidence, not resentment.

Asking for What Your Team Needs

Whether it is headcount, time for tech debt, a new tool, or sprint capacity for improvement work — the way you frame the ask determines whether you get it.

Framing Requests Effectively

  • Frame in business terms. "If we invest 2 sprints in test automation, we will reduce regression testing from 3 days to 3 hours per release, enabling us to release weekly instead of monthly." This is not a tech favour — it is a business investment with a measurable return.
  • Quantify the cost of inaction. "Last quarter, we spent 140 hours on manual regression testing. That is roughly 4 developer-weeks. The automation investment pays for itself in a single quarter." Numbers make the invisible visible.
  • Propose, don't demand. Give options with trade-offs: "Option A: full automation in 3 sprints, zero manual regression after. Option B: automate critical paths in 1 sprint, reduce manual effort by 60%. I recommend B as a starting point." This shows you are thinking about constraints, not just your wish list.
  • Anticipate objections. Think about what your manager will ask: "What if we need that capacity for feature X?" Have an answer ready: "We can phase it — 20% capacity per sprint instead of a dedicated sprint."

Giving Upward Feedback

Giving feedback to your manager is one of the more uncomfortable leadership skills, but it is also one of the most valuable. Most managers receive far less honest feedback than they think.

Making It Work

  • Use the SBI model. Situation → Behaviour → Impact, the same framework you would use for peer feedback. "In yesterday's planning session (S), when you overrode the team's estimate without discussion (B), it made the team feel their input doesn't matter (I)."
  • Pick the right moment. A private 1:1 is the place for this — never in front of the team or in a group setting. Timing matters as much as content.
  • Frame as partnership. "I want to help you help us" is a very different signal than "you are doing this wrong." You are on the same side. Make that obvious.
  • Be specific and actionable. "I think your communication could be better" is unhelpful. "When priorities shift, a quick Slack message explaining why would help the team adjust without feeling whiplashed" gives them something concrete to do differently.
💡

Remember: Your manager is also figuring things out. They have pressures you may not see, deadlines you may not know about, and a manager of their own to manage up to. Extending the same empathy upward that you extend to your team makes the whole chain work better.

Influence Without Authority

As you grow into leadership, you quickly discover that most of the people you need to align with do not report to you. Driving outcomes across teams, functions, and organisational boundaries requires a different set of skills than managing a team you directly lead.

Key Insight

Most engineering leaders don't have direct authority over everyone they need to influence. Product managers, other team leads, platform teams, stakeholders — you can't tell them what to do. You have to earn influence through credibility, relationships, and clear communication.

The 4 Currencies of Influence

Influence is not a single skill — it is built on four distinct foundations. Each one can be developed deliberately over time.

Technical Credibility

People listen to those who have demonstrated sound judgment. Build this by being right often, admitting when you're wrong, and showing your reasoning openly. One well-reasoned ADR does more for your credibility than a dozen opinions in Slack.

Relationship Capital

Invest in relationships before you need them. A 15-minute coffee chat with the platform team lead pays dividends when you need their help in a crunch. Know the people, understand their pressures, build genuine rapport.

Shared Context

People resist what they don't understand. Before asking someone to change their approach or prioritize your request, make sure they understand the full picture. Share data. Explain the "why." Give them the same context you have.

Reliable Follow-Through

Every commitment you keep builds your influence. Every one you break erodes it. The simplest way to build influence is to consistently do what you said you would do, when you said you would do it.

Influencing Across Teams

Cross-team collaboration is where influence is most tested. These five practices turn peer-level requests into productive partnerships.

Five Steps to Effective Cross-Team Influence

  1. Lead with their problem, not yours. Frame your request in terms of what the other team cares about. "This will reduce your on-call alerts by 40%" lands better than "We need you to change your API contract."
  2. Come with options, not demands. "Could we try X or Y?" opens doors. "We need you to do Z" closes them. Giving people choice creates ownership over the solution.
  3. Build coalitions. If 3 teams agree something matters, it's harder to ignore than if only you're asking. Alignment across multiple stakeholders creates momentum that a single voice cannot.
  4. Document agreements publicly. After a cross-team decision, send a summary to all parties. Shared written agreements prevent "I don't remember agreeing to that." A brief email or a shared document keeps everyone honest.
  5. Give credit generously. When a cross-team collaboration succeeds, make sure the other team gets visible credit. This makes them want to work with you again. Influence compounds when people associate working with you with good outcomes.

Escalation vs. Influence

One of the hardest judgment calls in cross-team work is knowing when to keep working the relationship and when to escalate. Get it wrong in either direction and you pay a price.

Common Pitfall

Escalating too quickly — going to your manager or their manager the moment you hit resistance. This burns relationship capital and signals that you can't handle peer-level negotiation. It also makes the other team defensive, which makes future collaboration harder.

Better Approach

Having a direct conversation first. "I think we see this differently. Can we grab 30 minutes to align? I want to understand your constraints." Most cross-team friction dissolves with a direct conversation. Save escalation for when you have genuinely exhausted peer-level options.

When to Influence vs. When to Escalate

Influence (handle it yourself) Escalate (involve leadership)
Disagreement on approach — different technical opinions Blocked for 2+ weeks with no movement
Competing priorities — your request vs theirs Commitment was made and repeatedly broken
Different interpretations of requirements Decision needed that neither of you has authority to make
Resistance to a new practice or tool Team is actively working against an agreed decision

Scripts for Building Influence

Influence across teams lives or dies in the specific words you choose. These scripts show how to ask for help and hold people accountable without damaging the relationship.

"Asking another team for help" ▼

Setting: Slack or meeting with a peer team lead.

"Hey [name], I need your help with something. We have been seeing [specific issue] that I think touches your team's [service/area]. I have done some investigation on our side and narrowed it down to [specifics]. I know your team is busy — could we grab 20 minutes this week to look at it together? I want to make sure we solve this in a way that works for both teams, not just push the problem your way."

Why it works: It leads with respect for their time, shows you have done your homework, and frames it as collaboration not delegation.

"When a cross-team agreement is not being honoured" ▼

Setting: Private conversation with the other team's lead.

"Hey [name], I wanted to check in about the [API change / migration / dependency] we discussed last month. We agreed on [specific thing] by [date], and it looks like it has slipped. I want to understand what happened — has something changed on your side? I am not trying to escalate this. I just want to figure out the right path forward together. If the timeline needs to shift, I would rather know now so we can adjust on our end."

Why it works: It references the specific agreement, asks for context instead of blaming, and explicitly says it is not an escalation — which keeps the relationship intact.

💡

Worth remembering: The leaders who have the most influence are rarely the loudest. They are the ones who listen well, follow through consistently, and make other people's work easier. Influence is built one interaction at a time.

Section 4

Driving Change

Spotting what needs to change is the easier part. Making change actually happen — in real teams, with real constraints and real human nature — is where leadership is tested. This section gives you frameworks for understanding why change stalls and how to push it through to lasting adoption.

Key Insight

When you propose a change and nothing happens, diagnose why before assuming resistance. People may be overburdened, overwhelmed, unclear on what you are asking, or lacking confidence — not resisting. The fix depends entirely on the diagnosis, and getting it wrong means wasting effort on the wrong intervention.

Why Change Doesn't Happen

There are six distinct reasons change stalls, and each one requires a fundamentally different response. Treating them all as "resistance" is like treating every illness with the same medicine.

Overload

Signal: "I agree, but I just don't have time right now."

This is usually legitimate. People are drowning in existing commitments, and asking them to take on more without removing something is setting them up to fail.

Fix: Create space before adding. Defer or remove something else. Make the change part of existing work, not an addition to it. If you cannot free up capacity, the change will not stick — no matter how good it is.

Lack of Clarity

Signal: "I'm not sure what to do differently."

The "why" landed, but the "what" and "how" did not. Abstract goals like "improve quality" do not give people anything to act on.

Fix: Be painfully specific, with examples. Not "write better tests" but "every PR should include at least one happy-path test and one error-case test. Here is an example from last week's PR that shows what I mean."

Lack of Motivation

Signal: Silent compliance. Going through the motions without conviction.

The change has not connected to anything the person cares about. They do not see how it helps them, their career, or the team's daily experience.

Fix: Connect the change to what they care about. Less firefighting, fewer weekend deploys, career growth, building something they are proud of. Use real data and stories, not abstract arguments about "best practices."

Lack of Confidence

Signal: Avoidance. "I'll try..." followed by nothing happening. Hesitation.

They want to change but are not sure they can do it well. They may be embarrassed to admit it, especially if others seem to be adapting easily.

Fix: Teach first, then expect. Pair with them. Provide examples they can follow. Give a safe space to practise without judgment. Celebrate effort, not just outcomes.

Disagreement

Signal: Explicit pushback. Counter-arguments. Visible reluctance.

This is actual resistance — and it may be well-founded. The person may see trade-offs you have not considered or have context from past failures.

Fix: Listen genuinely. Ask what concerns them. Adjust the approach if their feedback is valid. If you still believe in the change, make the case clearly and ask for commitment with a trial period: "Let's try for 3 sprints and measure."

Habit

Signal: People agree, start well, then gradually drift back to the old way.

Old habits are neurologically strong. Without sustained reinforcement, people default to what is familiar — not because they disagree, but because the new way has not become automatic yet.

Fix: Consistent nudging for 4–6 weeks. Build the change into tools: checklists, PR templates, linter rules, automated checks. Make the new way the path of least resistance. Automate enforcement where possible.

7 Steps to Drive Lasting Change

A Framework for Making Change Stick

  1. Make the case. Explain why the change matters in terms the team cares about. Not "industry best practice says..." but "last quarter we had 4 production incidents caused by X, and this change directly addresses the root cause."
  2. Make it specific. Translate the abstract into the concrete. "Improve code review quality" is vague. "Every review comment should reference a specific line, explain why it matters, and suggest a fix" is actionable.
  3. Make it small. Start with the smallest version of the change that produces a visible result. A pilot with one team. A trial for two sprints. Small wins build evidence and momentum for larger shifts.
  4. Make space. If you are adding something, remove or simplify something else. Change requires cognitive and calendar capacity, and most teams are already running at full load.
  5. Model it yourself. Be the most visible adopter. If you are asking the team to write better commit messages, make yours exemplary. If you are asking for more thorough code reviews, your reviews should be the gold standard. People follow behaviour, not directives.
  6. Reinforce relentlessly. Mention it in standups. Celebrate people who adopt it. Give gentle feedback when it slips. The first 4–6 weeks are critical — most changes die from lack of follow-through, not lack of initial buy-in.
  7. Measure and share results. Track the impact and share it: "Since we started doing X, our production incidents dropped from 4 per month to 1." Data sustains motivation long after the initial enthusiasm fades.

When to Push vs. When to Pause

Knowing when to persist and when to step back is a nuanced leadership skill. This table can help calibrate your approach:

Push Harder When... Pause and Reassess When...
The team understands the why but is slow to start Multiple people are pushing back with specific, valid concerns
Early adopters are seeing positive results The change is creating more problems than it solves
Resistance is mostly habit and inertia You cannot clearly articulate the expected benefit
You have evidence that it works elsewhere The team is already overwhelmed with other changes
Not changing has a clear, measurable cost You are the only person who believes in this change
You have made the change small and specific You have been pushing for weeks with zero traction

Handling Specific Situations

"We've tried this before" ▼

This is change fatigue, and it deserves respect. Ask: "What happened last time? What went wrong?" Often the previous attempt failed because of poor timing, lack of follow-through, or missing support — not because the idea itself was bad.

Acknowledge the history honestly, then articulate what is different this time: different context, a clearer plan, a commitment to sustained follow-through. If you cannot explain what is different, consider that they may have a point worth listening to.

"I don't have time" ▼

This is usually a legitimate capacity issue, not an excuse. People are genuinely stretched. The fix is not to push harder — it is to make space. Ask: "What could I take off your plate to make room for this?" or "How can we integrate this into work you are already doing?"

If the change truly matters, it has to be prioritised like any other work — with something else explicitly deprioritised to create room. Piling it on top of an already-full workload guarantees failure.

"The current way works fine" ▼

This is comfort zone resistance, and the fix is making the hidden cost visible. "Works fine" often means "I have not noticed the pain because I have adapted to it." The data makes it real.

Share concrete evidence: "Our current approach cost us 12 hours of rework last sprint" or "Teams using the new approach are deploying 3x more frequently with half the incidents." If you do not have data, get some before pushing the change. You might even discover they are right and the current way actually is fine.

People agree but don't actually change ▼

This is the most common failure mode and it is almost always a habit problem. The gap between "I agree" and "I consistently do it" is enormous. Agreement is cognitive; behaviour is habitual.

Close the gap by making the new behaviour the path of least resistance. Automate enforcement where possible: linter rules, PR templates, CI checks, automated reminders. Build the change into the tools and processes people already use every day. And follow up — consistently, kindly, persistently — for at least 4–6 weeks.

One person consistently blocks changes ▼

Start by understanding why. Are they raising legitimate concerns? Do they have context others lack? Sometimes the "blocker" is the most thoughtful person in the room, and their objections deserve genuine engagement.

If, after honest listening, their objections do not hold up: have a direct conversation. "I value your input, and I have genuinely considered your concerns. Here is why I believe we should move forward. I need your commitment to give this a fair try for the next 3 sprints." If blocking continues after that, it becomes a performance and expectations conversation, not a change management one.

💡

Patience matters: Real behavioural change takes 4–6 weeks of sustained, visible effort. Most change initiatives die in week 2 when enthusiasm fades and old habits reassert themselves. The difference between teams that actually improve and those that don't is persistence through that uncomfortable middle period.

💜

Empathy check: Before concluding that someone is resistant, rule out the other five causes. Are they overloaded? Do they understand what you are asking? Do they know how to do it? Do they believe it matters? Do they just need more time to build the habit? The answer tells you what kind of support they actually need.

Challenging the Status Quo

Healthy teams are never fully satisfied with "this is how we do it." Not because they are restless, but because they understand that the environment is always changing — and processes that were optimal last year may be holding them back today.

Key Insight

The world is changing fast — AI is reshaping how we write code, test, and deploy. Frameworks evolve, patterns that were right 3 years ago may not be right today. Teams that regularly question "how we have always done it" adapt and thrive. Teams that don't will find themselves solving yesterday's problems with yesterday's tools while the landscape moves on without them.

The Quarterly "Why Do We Still Do This?" Review

Set aside an hour each quarter to honestly examine your team's assumptions. These five questions can surface improvement opportunities that are hiding in plain sight:

Five Questions for Quarterly Review

  • Are our processes still fit for purpose? What made sense when the team was 4 people may not work at 12. What was right for monthly releases may not suit continuous delivery. Processes should evolve with the team, not calcify.
  • What manual work could be automated? Code generation, test scaffolding, documentation, code review triage — the tooling landscape, particularly around AI, is evolving faster than most teams realise. Revisit this every quarter because the answer keeps changing.
  • Are our patterns keeping up with language and framework evolution? Languages and frameworks introduce new capabilities and deprecate old approaches. If your codebase still looks like it was written three versions ago, you are carrying unnecessary complexity and missing opportunities for simplification.
  • What would we do differently if we started fresh today? This question cuts through institutional inertia. You may not be able to start from scratch, but the answer reveals which incremental improvements would have the highest impact.
  • What are other teams doing that we are not? Look outward. Read engineering blogs. Talk to peers at other companies. Attend meetups. The best ideas frequently come from outside your immediate circle.

Areas to Continuously Challenge

Delivery Process

Are sprints still the right cadence, or would continuous flow work better for your team? Are standups serving the team, or just a ritual everyone endures? Is your planning process helping you deliver, or consuming time that could be spent building?

Ask yourself: "If we designed our delivery process from scratch today, how much of the current process would survive?"

Development Workflow

Are you leveraging AI tools effectively for code generation, test creation, documentation, and review? Is your CI/CD pipeline as fast as it could be? Are your development environments helping or creating friction?

Ask yourself: "What part of our daily workflow still feels like unnecessary friction?"

Architecture Patterns

Are your current patterns the best choice for today's requirements, or are they inherited from a different era when the context was different? Team capabilities grow, infrastructure evolves, and what was a pragmatic compromise may now be an unnecessary constraint.

Ask yourself: "Are we using this pattern because it is the best choice, or because it is what we know?"

Testing Strategy

Are you testing the right things at the right levels? Is your test suite giving you genuine confidence in deployments, or just generating coverage numbers? Are you investing time in tests that rarely catch bugs while under-testing the areas that actually break?

Ask yourself: "Where was our last production bug? Would our current test suite have caught it?"

Communication

Are your meetings, documentation, and channels serving the team well? Is information reaching the right people at the right time? Are decisions being captured and made discoverable, or vanishing into Slack threads and forgotten meeting notes?

Ask yourself: "If a new team member joined today, how long would it take them to understand how we work and why?"

🌱

Innovation does not require permission. If you see a better way, propose it with evidence. Run a small experiment on your own time or with willing teammates. Share the results. The best improvements often start as informal experiments by curious people, not as top-down mandates.

Section 6

Written Artifacts That Drive Decisions

Engineers communicate as much through written documents as through meetings. Design docs, RFCs, and Architecture Decision Records are not bureaucracy — they are tools for aligning teams, capturing reasoning, and making decisions durable. Learning to write and review these well is one of the most overlooked leadership skills.

Key Insight

The best engineering decisions are not made in meetings — they are made in documents that were circulated before the meeting. When people have time to read, think, and comment asynchronously, the quality of the decision goes up and the length of the meeting goes down.

Three Artifacts Every Engineering Leader Should Know

Design Doc

Used when: starting a new feature, significant refactor, or system change.

Contains: problem statement, proposed solution, alternatives considered, trade-offs, risks, and a request for feedback.

Length: 2–5 pages. The goal is alignment, not perfection.

"The best design docs are written before the first line of code, not after."

RFC (Request for Comments)

Used when: proposing a change that affects multiple teams or establishes a new standard.

Contains: motivation, detailed proposal, migration plan, and open questions. The "request" framing matters — it signals that feedback is genuinely wanted, not just tolerated.

"An RFC that receives no comments was either perfect or unread. Bet on the latter."

ADR (Architecture Decision Record)

Used when: making a significant technical decision that future engineers will question.

Contains: context, decision, consequences, and status. ADRs are short (1 page) and stored in the repo alongside code.

"The goal is not to justify the decision to today's team — it is to explain it to the engineer who joins in 18 months and wonders why."

The Design Doc Template

A Structure for Capturing Decisions

  1. Title and Date — What are we deciding? When?
  2. Authors — Who is driving this? (Ownership, not just attribution)
  3. Status — Draft / Under Review / Accepted / Superseded
  4. Problem Statement — What problem are we solving? Why now? (2–3 sentences. Use the Issue-Impact-Action structure from Section 1.)
  5. Proposed Solution — What do you recommend? How does it work? Include enough detail for someone to evaluate the approach, but not so much that it reads like implementation docs.
  6. Alternatives Considered — What else did you evaluate? Why did you reject them? This is the most important section — it shows your thinking, prevents others from re-proposing rejected approaches, and builds confidence in the recommendation.
  7. Trade-offs and Risks — What are you giving up? What could go wrong? Being upfront about downsides builds trust.
  8. Open Questions — What do you still need input on? This gives reviewers a clear invitation to contribute.
  9. Decision — Once accepted: what was decided, by whom, and when.
Common Pitfall

Writing the design doc after the implementation is done. The code is already merged, the doc is a formality, and nobody reviews it because the decision has already been made. The doc exists to satisfy process, not to improve the outcome.

Better Approach

Writing the design doc before coding starts. Circulate it with a clear feedback deadline. Hold a 30-minute review meeting where the only agenda is the doc. Make design doc review as normal as code review.

How to Review a Design Doc

Five Questions That Make Reviews Count

  1. "Does the problem statement match what I understand the problem to be?" — If not, the rest of the doc is solving the wrong thing.
  2. "Were the alternatives genuinely considered, or is this a justification for a predetermined choice?" — The alternatives section reveals the quality of thinking.
  3. "What could go wrong that the author has not mentioned?" — Your job as reviewer is to stress-test, not rubber-stamp.
  4. "Will I be able to explain this decision to someone in 6 months using only this document?" — If not, it needs more context.
  5. "What questions would a new team member have?" — Fresh-eyes thinking catches assumptions the author cannot see.
💡

Start with one artifact. If your team writes no design docs today, mandate them only for changes that touch 3+ services or take longer than a sprint. That alone will catch 80% of the decisions that need documentation. Expand from there.
