Your First 90 Days as a Leader

A practical playbook for your transition into leadership — what to do in your first week, first month, and first quarter.

Section 1

The Mindset Shift

The day you become a leader, everything changes — and nothing changes. You still sit with the same people, work on the same codebase, attend the same standups. But your job is fundamentally different. Your success is no longer measured by what you produce. It is measured by what the team produces, how they grow, and whether the systems you build outlast your involvement.

Key Insight

You were promoted because you were an exceptional IC. The hardest part of the transition is accepting that the skills that got you here are not the skills that will make you successful in the new role. Writing the best code, solving the hardest bugs, knowing the most about the system — these are now secondary. Your primary job is enabling others to do these things.

What Changes When You Become a Leader

Dimension As an IC As a Leader
Your success metric "I shipped it" "The team shipped it with quality"
Your time allocation 80% coding 40% enabling, 30% strategic, 20% coding, 10% learning
Your daily question "What will I build today?" "What is blocking my team today?"
Your hardest moment "This bug is impossible" "This conversation is uncomfortable"
Your biggest temptation "I will stay late to finish" "I will just do it myself — it is faster"
Your measure of a good day "I wrote clean, working code" "Someone on the team grew because of something I did"
💡

This shift does not happen overnight. It takes most leaders 3–6 months to fully let go of the IC identity. Be patient with yourself. The discomfort you feel is not a sign that you are failing — it is a sign that you are growing.

Section 2

Phase 1: Listen (Week 1–2)

Your first two weeks have one purpose: understand the terrain before you try to change it. Every conversation, every meeting, every observation is an input. You are building a mental map of the team — its strengths, its pain points, its dynamics, and its unspoken rules.

The 1:1 Discovery Conversation

In your first week, schedule a 1:1 with every team member. These are not status updates. They are discovery conversations. Ask these seven questions — and listen more than you talk.

7 Questions for Your First 1:1s

  1. "What is going well on the team that I should make sure to protect?"

    This reveals what works and signals that you will not recklessly change things.

  2. "What is the most frustrating part of your day-to-day work?"

    This surfaces the real pain points, not the ones in Jira.

  3. "If you could change one thing about how the team works, what would it be?"

    This gives you their priorities, not yours.

  4. "What do you wish leadership understood better about your work?"

    This reveals gaps between the team and management that you can bridge.

  5. "Who do you go to when you are stuck? Who goes to you?"

    This maps the real knowledge network, which is rarely the org chart.

  6. "What has been tried before that did not work?"

    This prevents you from repeating past failures and shows respect for the team's history.

  7. "What should I know about working with this team that nobody will tell me directly?"

    This invites the unfiltered truth. Not everyone will answer honestly, but some will — and what they share is gold.

⚠

Take notes after every conversation — not during. Writing during a 1:1 makes people guarded. Write your notes immediately after. Look for patterns across conversations. If 4 out of 6 people mention the same frustration, you have found your first problem worth solving.

Section 3

Phase 2: Learn (Month 1)

With your listening done, month 1 is about building deep understanding. You are connecting what people told you with what the data shows, what the code reveals, and what the processes look like in practice — not just on paper.

Four Things to Map

The Knowledge Map

Who knows what? Where are the single points of failure? If one person left tomorrow, what knowledge would disappear? Draw it out. This is your bus-factor analysis. The areas with the thinnest knowledge coverage are your highest risks.

The Delivery Health Map

How long do stories take from start to done? Where do things get stuck? Are MRs sitting in review for days? Is the CI pipeline reliable? Look at the sprint board history for the last 3 sprints. The patterns tell you more than any individual told you in 1:1s.

The Stakeholder Map

Who are your stakeholders? What do they care about most? Who has the authority to change your team's priorities? Who needs regular updates? Draw the relationships. Understanding the stakeholder landscape early prevents surprises later.

The Culture Map

What are the unwritten rules? How does the team actually make decisions (vs. how it says it makes decisions)? Who has informal influence? What gets rewarded, and what gets ignored? Culture is what happens when nobody is looking — and understanding it is critical before you try to change it.

The Month 1 Diagnosis

Writing Your First Assessment

After 4 weeks of listening and learning, write down:

  1. The team's top 3 strengths — Protect these. Do not accidentally break what works.
  2. The team's top 3 problems — The ones that came up repeatedly across conversations AND that you can see in the data.
  3. The one problem you will tackle first — And why this one, not the others.
  4. What you still do not understand — And how you will fill that gap.

Share this with your manager first for alignment, then with the team for validation: "Here is what I have observed. Does this match your experience? Am I missing anything?"

Common Pitfall: Confirmation Bias

Forming your opinion in week 1 and spending month 1 looking for evidence to confirm it. This is the most common mistake new leaders make. You hear one strong voice in your first 1:1 and anchor your entire understanding to that person's perspective.

Better Approach: Seek Disconfirming Evidence

Actively look for disconfirming evidence. If 5 people say the process is fine but 1 says it is broken, investigate the 1. If your gut says the team is slow, check the data before acting on it. The truth is usually more nuanced than any single perspective.

Section 4

Phase 3: Act (Month 2–3)

You have listened, you have learned, and you have identified the most important problem. Now it is time to act — but strategically. Your first change sets the tone for your leadership. Choose wisely.

The First Win Criteria

5 Qualities of a Good First Initiative

Your first initiative should meet as many of these criteria as possible:

  1. Visible — The team and stakeholders can see the improvement. A process change nobody notices does not build credibility.
  2. Achievable in 2–4 weeks — Not a multi-month project. You need a win, not a work-in-progress.
  3. Broadly felt — It improves something that most of the team cares about. Fixing one person's pet peeve does not build team-wide trust.
  4. Connected to what you heard — It addresses a frustration that came up in your 1:1s. This shows you listened and acted on what you heard.
  5. Low risk — Your first change should not be controversial. Save the bold moves for after you have earned trust.

Good First Wins

These are examples of first wins that new leaders have used successfully. Each one is small enough to achieve in a few weeks, visible enough to build credibility, and connected to common team frustrations.

Fix the Flaky CI Pipeline

If the team mentioned it as a daily frustration, fixing it shows you listened and can deliver. Tangible, visible, and everyone benefits.

Introduce a Definition of "Done"

If work is ambiguous about when it is complete, a shared checklist removes friction and prevents rework. Quick to implement, immediate impact.

Start Meaningful 1:1s

If the previous lead did not do them (or only did status updates), introducing growth-oriented 1:1s signals that you care about people, not just tickets.

Create an MR Review Checklist

If reviews are inconsistent, a shared checklist raises the bar immediately and creates a teaching tool for every review.

Set Up a Team Dashboard

If nobody knows the team's health at a glance, a simple dashboard (deployment frequency, open MRs, incident count) makes the invisible visible.

Run a Structured Retro

If retros have become stale or nonexistent, facilitating one good retrospective with real follow-through shows the team that improvement is now a priority.

The 30/60/90 Day Checkpoint

What Success Looks Like at Each Stage

  1. Day 30:

    You can describe the team's top 3 strengths and top 3 problems. You have met all stakeholders. You have a first win identified and in progress. The team does not feel disrupted.

  2. Day 60:

    Your first win is delivered. You have established a regular 1:1 rhythm. You can explain the team's delivery health with data. Stakeholders know your name and trust you to communicate honestly.

  3. Day 90:

    The team feels heard. At least one process or practice has measurably improved. You have a plan for the next quarter. Your manager has confidence in your direction. You have started to delegate deliberately, not just by default.

💡

Share your 30/60/90 plan with your manager in week 2. This creates alignment, gives them confidence that you have a plan, and opens the door for early feedback. Do not wait until day 90 to find out you were focused on the wrong things.

Section 5

Building Key Relationships

Leadership is relational. Your effectiveness depends not just on what you know, but on the trust you build with the people around you. In your first 90 days, there are four relationships that will make or break your transition.

The Four Critical Relationships

Your Manager

Align on expectations early: "What does success look like for me at 30, 60, 90 days?" Establish a communication rhythm (weekly 1:1 at minimum). Surface problems early. Ask: "What is the one thing you most need from me?" Their answer tells you what they are being measured on — and therefore what you should prioritise.

Your Team

Trust is built through consistency and follow-through, not grand gestures. Do what you say you will do. Show up to 1:1s on time. Follow up on things people raised. Do not play favourites. The team is watching everything you do in month 1 — they are deciding whether to trust you.

Your Stakeholders

Introduce yourself proactively. Ask: "What are your priorities? How do you prefer to receive updates? What frustrates you about engineering communication?" Then deliver exactly that. Stakeholders who trust you give the team more autonomy. Stakeholders who do not trust you start micromanaging.

Peer Leaders

Other tech leads and engineering managers are your most valuable support network. They understand your challenges in a way nobody else does. Invest in these relationships early — a 15-minute coffee chat with a peer lead pays dividends for months.

Scripts for First 90 Days Conversations

These are not word-for-word scripts you must follow — they are starting points that show the tone and approach. Adapt them to your own voice and the specific situation.

First 1:1 with a team member ▼

Setting: Your very first 1:1 with each person.

"I wanted to take this time to get to know you and understand your experience on the team. I am not coming in with a plan to change everything — I want to learn first. So I have some questions, but honestly, the most useful thing for me is just hearing your perspective. What is working well on this team? What frustrates you? And if you had a magic wand, what is one thing you would change?"

First conversation with your manager ▼

Setting: Your first week, aligning on expectations.

"I want to make sure we are aligned on what success looks like for me. From your perspective, what are the most important outcomes for the team in the next quarter? What should I focus on in my first 30 days? And is there anything you are worried about that I should have on my radar? I would also like to set up a regular cadence with you — I think a weekly check-in would help me stay aligned, especially in these early weeks."

Introducing yourself to a stakeholder ▼

Setting: First meeting with a product manager or business stakeholder.

"I am the new lead for the team. I wanted to introduce myself and understand your priorities. What are the most important things your team needs from us right now? How do you prefer to receive updates — would a weekly summary work, or do you prefer a different format? And is there anything about how the team has communicated in the past that you would like to see change? I want to make sure our collaboration works well from the start."

When someone tests your authority early ▼

Setting: A meeting or Slack thread where someone pushes back hard on your role.

"I appreciate you being direct. I want to hear your perspective — can you walk me through your concerns? I am not here to pull rank or override decisions that the team has made thoughtfully. But I do need to understand the reasoning so I can represent us well to stakeholders. Let us work through this together."

Section 6

Setting Up Your Operating Rhythm

Leadership is not a series of heroic moments. It is a rhythm of consistent, small practices that compound over time. Setting up this rhythm early gives you structure when everything else feels chaotic.

Your Weekly Leadership Rhythm

By the end of month 1, aim to have this rhythm in place. It does not need to be perfect — it needs to be consistent.

The Weekly Cadence

  1. Monday:

    Review the sprint board. Identify blockers. Prepare for any stakeholder updates.

  2. Daily:

    A 5-minute glance at team channels and MR activity. Not to micromanage — to stay aware.

  3. 1:1s:

    Spread across the week. Fortnightly for each person (weekly for anyone ramping up or struggling). Use the 3-part structure: their agenda, your feedback, growth focus.

  4. Wednesday or Thursday:

    Mid-sprint check on delivery health. Are we on track? Any risks to surface?

  5. Friday (10 min):

    Personal reflection. What went well? What would I do differently? Who do I need to check in with next week?

Rhythm vs. Chaos

With Rhythm Without Rhythm
You know the team's health before standup You find out about problems in standup
Stakeholders get proactive updates Stakeholders chase you for information
1:1s happen consistently, trust builds 1:1s get cancelled, people feel unimportant
You have time for strategic thinking Every day is reactive firefighting
Problems surface early and small Problems surface late and large
Section 7

The Emotional Side

Nobody talks about the emotional weight of becoming a leader for the first time. The imposter syndrome, the loneliness of being between IC and management, the anxiety of not knowing if you are doing it right. These feelings are normal. Every leader you admire has felt them.

Imposter Syndrome

"Everyone else seems to know what they are doing. I am faking it." You are not faking it. You were chosen because someone saw leadership potential in you. The uncertainty you feel is not incompetence — it is the natural discomfort of learning a new role. Every senior leader you know felt this way in their first months.

The Identity Shift

You used to be the person who solved the hardest problems. Now your job is to let others solve them. This feels like losing a part of yourself. It is not. It is evolving. The pride you used to get from writing great code will be replaced by the pride of watching someone you mentored write great code. It takes time. Let it.

The Loneliness

You are no longer "one of the team" in the same way. People filter what they say to you. You cannot vent to your team the way you used to vent to your peers. Find outlets: a mentor, a peer lead, a manager you trust. You need someone you can be honest with about how hard this is.

The Fear of Getting It Wrong

You will get things wrong. You will give feedback that does not land well. You will make a decision that turns out to be suboptimal. You will miss a signal. This is not failure — it is leadership. The question is not whether you will make mistakes, but whether you will learn from them quickly and openly.

💡

Find a mentor — someone who has been through this transition before. Not necessarily your manager. A peer lead, a senior engineer who moved into management, or someone outside the company. Having one person you can ask "is this normal?" changes everything.

Section 8

Common Traps and How to Avoid Them

These are the patterns that derail new leaders most often. Recognising them in advance will not make you immune, but it will help you catch yourself earlier.

Fixing Everything at Once

You see ten things that could be better and try to change all of them simultaneously. The result: you lose credibility, exhaust the team, and nothing gets finished. Change fatigue is real. Pick one thing, do it well, then move to the next.

Ask yourself: "If I could only fix one thing in the next 30 days, which one would create the most trust and momentum?"

Being the Hero Coder

You default to what is comfortable — taking on the hardest stories, writing the most code, being the top contributor on the commit graph. It feels productive, but you are doing IC work while leadership work goes undone. Your team does not need another coder. They need a leader.

Ask yourself: "Am I coding because the team needs me to, or because it makes me feel useful?"

Avoiding Hard Conversations

You notice a performance issue or a team dynamic problem but convince yourself it will sort itself out. It will not. Problems left unaddressed in month 1 become crises by month 3. Having a respectful, honest conversation early is always easier than having a difficult one later.

Ask yourself: "Am I avoiding this conversation because it is unnecessary, or because it is uncomfortable?"

Not Asking for Help

You feel like you should have all the answers because you are "the leader" now. You do not. Nobody does. Find a mentor — someone who has been through this transition before. Talk to peer leaders on other teams. Ask your own manager for guidance. The strongest leaders are the ones who know what they do not know and seek help actively.

Ask yourself: "Who can I learn from? Who has navigated this transition before me?"

Trying to Be Everyone's Friend

You want the team to like you, so you avoid setting boundaries, saying no, or holding people accountable. The result: you are liked but not respected, and the team's standards drift. Leadership requires being kind and honest, not just kind.

Ask yourself: "Am I optimising for being liked, or for the team's long-term health?"

Copying the Previous Leader

You either try to be exactly like the previous lead (because it worked for them) or the exact opposite (because you disagreed with their style). Neither works. You are a different person leading in a different moment. Take what was good, leave what was not, and lead as yourself.

Ask yourself: "Am I making decisions based on what this team needs, or based on what the previous lead would or would not have done?"

Saying Yes to Everything

Stakeholders ask for features, your manager asks for reports, the team asks for process changes. You say yes to all of it because you want to prove yourself. Your calendar fills, your capacity collapses, and nothing gets done well. Learning to say "not this sprint, but here is when we can" is a core leadership skill.

Ask yourself: "If I say yes to this, what am I implicitly saying no to?"

Neglecting Your Own Growth

You spend all your energy helping others grow and forget that you are also learning a new skill. Block time for reading, reflecting, and learning leadership. It is not selfish — it is necessary. A leader who stops growing stops leading.

Ask yourself: "When did I last invest time in my own development as a leader?"
