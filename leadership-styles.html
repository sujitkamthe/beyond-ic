Know Your Leadership Style

Understanding how you are wired — and how the people around you are wired — is the foundation of adaptive leadership. This is not about labels. It is about self-awareness that leads to better communication, delegation, feedback, and team composition.

Section 1

Discover Your Style

Before you can adapt your leadership to others, you need to understand yourself. Most leaders default to leading in the style that feels natural to them — which works brilliantly for people who are wired similarly, and lands poorly for everyone else.

Key Insight

Leadership is the art of inspiring others to deliberately create an outcome that would not have otherwise been accomplished. But here is the thing: different people are inspired by different things. What motivates you may frustrate someone else. Understanding your own wiring is the first step to understanding theirs.

The Quick Self-Assessment

4 Questions to Find Your Default Style

For each question, pick the answer that resonates most naturally — not the one you think is "correct."

  1. When faced with a problem, I naturally:
    • (a) Analyse the data and research thoroughly
    • (b) Make a quick decision and act
    • (c) Talk it through with people and brainstorm
    • (d) Consider how it affects the team and seek consensus
  2. In meetings, I tend to:
    • (a) Listen carefully and contribute when I have something precise to say
    • (b) Drive toward a decision and get impatient with long discussions
    • (c) Generate energy and ideas, sometimes going on tangents
    • (d) Make sure everyone has been heard before moving on
  3. I get frustrated when:
    • (a) Decisions are made without proper analysis
    • (b) Progress is slow and people overthink things
    • (c) The atmosphere is flat and nobody is excited about the work
    • (d) There is conflict or people are being steamrolled
  4. My biggest blind spot is probably:
    • (a) I can be too cautious and miss the window to act
    • (b) I can be too blunt and not realise how my directness lands
    • (c) I can start many things and finish few
    • (d) I avoid necessary conflict to keep the peace
💡

If you answered mostly (a), you lean toward Analytical Thinker. Mostly (b), toward Driver. Mostly (c), toward Connector. Mostly (d), toward Supporter. Most people are a blend of two adjacent styles. There are no wrong answers — every style has genuine strengths.

Section 2

The 4 Styles in Depth

This framework is rooted in Carl Jung's work on personality types, adapted through models like Insights Discovery and DISC. It maps people along two dimensions: task-oriented vs. people-oriented, and introverted vs. extraverted. The result is four broad styles — each with distinct strengths, needs, and shadow sides.

The Analytical Thinker (Blue Energy)

Core drive: accuracy, depth, getting it right.

Strengths: thoroughness, quality focus, structured thinking, spotting risks others miss.

Communication preference: written over verbal, data over anecdotes, time to process before responding.

What energises them: solving complex problems, building elegant systems, getting to the root cause.

What drains them: rushed decisions, vague requirements, meetings with no agenda, being put on the spot.

Shadow side: analysis paralysis, perfectionism, can seem cold or distant when they are actually thinking deeply.

Giving feedback: be specific, fact-based, and written when possible. "In the last 3 MRs, test coverage averaged 62% against our 80% target" lands better than "your testing needs improvement."

The Driver (Red Energy)

Core drive: results, speed, getting it done.

Strengths: decisiveness, ownership, cutting through ambiguity, moving fast under pressure.

Communication preference: direct and brief, bottom line first, no preamble.

What energises them: clear goals, autonomy, visible progress, winning.

What drains them: bureaucracy, indecision, long meetings that go nowhere, having to explain the same thing twice.

Shadow side: can be blunt or dismissive, may steamroll quieter voices, prioritises speed over people's feelings.

Giving feedback: be direct, do not soften. "The stakeholder meeting went off track because we did not prepare the data. Here is what I suggest for next time." They respect honesty, not diplomacy.

The Connector (Yellow Energy)

Core drive: relationships, energy, making things exciting.

Strengths: brainstorming, team morale, connecting people across silos, generating enthusiasm for new ideas.

Communication preference: verbal over written, stories over spreadsheets, collaborative over solo.

What energises them: new ideas, recognition, creative freedom, group energy.

What drains them: repetitive tasks, working alone for long stretches, excessive process, being ignored.

Shadow side: may overcommit, struggle with follow-through, get distracted by the next shiny idea before finishing the current one.

Giving feedback: lead with the positive, then be specific. "Your energy in the demo was great — stakeholders loved it. One thing to tighten: the timeline slide had gaps. Let us work on that together."

The Supporter (Green Energy)

Core drive: harmony, trust, making sure people are okay.

Strengths: team cohesion, reliability, mediating disputes, steady delivery, deep loyalty.

Communication preference: personal, warm, one-on-one over group settings, needs psychological safety.

What energises them: helping others succeed, stable teams, meaningful work, being trusted.

What drains them: conflict, sudden changes, feeling unappreciated, competitive environments.

Shadow side: may avoid necessary confrontation, struggle to say no, can be slow to adapt to change because they prioritise stability.

Giving feedback: create a safe 1:1 space. "I value how much you care about the team. I have also noticed you hold back in design discussions — and your perspective would make those conversations better. What would help you feel more comfortable speaking up?"

Section 3

Your Growth Direction

Every style has a natural growth edge — the complementary energy that, when developed, makes you a more complete leader. This is not about changing who you are. It is about expanding your range so you can lead effectively in situations that do not play to your natural strengths.

The Growth Map

  1. Analytical Thinker → Develop Connector energy

    Your growth edge is speed and warmth. Practice: make one decision this week with 80% data instead of waiting for 100%. In your next team meeting, start with a personal check-in instead of going straight to the agenda. Celebrate a win publicly, even if your instinct says the work speaks for itself.

  2. Driver → Develop Supporter energy

    Your growth edge is patience and empathy. Practice: in your next 1:1, spend the first 10 minutes only listening, not solving. When someone proposes an approach you disagree with, ask "help me understand your thinking" before sharing yours. After a tough sprint, acknowledge the effort before discussing what went wrong.

  3. Connector → Develop Analytical energy

    Your growth edge is follow-through and depth. Practice: before starting something new this week, finish one thing you started last week. Write down your next proposal with data, alternatives, and trade-offs before presenting it verbally. Set a personal rule: for every idea you generate, pick one to execute fully.

  4. Supporter → Develop Driver energy

    Your growth edge is assertiveness and directness. Practice: in the next team discussion, share your opinion before the group consensus forms — even if it is uncomfortable. Give one piece of constructive feedback this week that you have been holding back. When you disagree with a decision, say so in the meeting rather than accepting quietly and worrying later.

Key Insight

Growth does not mean abandoning your core style. An Analytical Thinker who develops Connector warmth does not stop being thorough — they become thorough AND approachable. A Driver who develops Supporter patience does not stop being decisive — they become decisive AND empathetic. The goal is expansion, not replacement.

💡

Ask someone you trust: "What is one thing I could do differently that would make me easier to work with?" The answer almost always points toward your growth edge.

Section 4

The Maturity Spectrum

Every style has three versions: immature, well-developed, and overdone. Understanding where you sit on this spectrum — honestly — is one of the most powerful self-awareness exercises a leader can do.

Style Immature Well-Developed Overdone
Analytical Thinker Nitpicky, indecisive, focused on irrelevant details, avoids commitments Precise, structured, quality-oriented, sees risks early, makes data-driven decisions Perfectionist, dismissive of others' ideas, cold, uses analysis to avoid action
Driver Aggressive, impatient, steamrolls others, cuts corners Decisive, action-oriented, accountable, clears obstacles, holds high standards Domineering, intimidating, creates fear rather than respect, burns people out
Connector Scattered, overcommits, seeks attention, avoids hard work, all talk Creative, builds energy, connects people, generates momentum, makes work exciting Chaotic, unreliable, avoids accountability, distracts from focus, all inspiration and no execution
Supporter Passive, avoids all conflict, adapts too easily, says yes to everything, invisible Empathetic, builds trust, stabilises teams, mediates disputes, loyal and reliable Conflict-averse to the point of harm, enables underperformance, resists necessary change, loses themselves in others' needs

Key Insight

The difference between "well-developed" and "overdone" is self-awareness. Every strength, pushed too far, becomes a weakness. The Analytical Thinker's thoroughness becomes paralysis. The Driver's decisiveness becomes bulldozing. The question is not "is my style good or bad?" — it is "am I using my style intentionally, or is it using me?"

⚠

If you read the "overdone" column and recognise yourself, that is a sign of self-awareness, not a problem. The first step to growth is honest recognition. The leaders who struggle most are the ones who read the overdone column and think "that is not me" when everyone around them knows it is.

Section 5

Building a Balanced Team

The best teams are not made up of people who think the same way. They are made up of complementary styles that balance each other's blind spots. As a leader, understanding your team's composition helps you assign work, form pairs, and anticipate friction.

What Happens When a Style Is Missing or Dominant

All Drivers, No Supporters

The team ships fast but burns out. People leave. Conflicts escalate because nobody de-escalates. The pace is unsustainable and morale craters.

All Analytical Thinkers, No Connectors

The code is beautiful but the team is silent. Nobody celebrates wins, energy is low, and new ideas die in committee. Everything is correct but nothing is exciting.

All Connectors, No Analytical Thinkers

Brainstorming sessions are amazing. Execution is poor. Ideas multiply faster than they ship. There is enthusiasm without rigour.

All Supporters, No Drivers

The team is harmonious but slow. Decisions take too long because nobody wants to push. Mediocrity is tolerated because confrontation is avoided.

Complementary Pairing

Use these pairings to get the best of both styles when assigning work together:

  • Analytical Thinker + Driver for technical spikes — depth meets speed. The Analytical Thinker ensures rigour while the Driver keeps momentum.
  • Connector + Analytical Thinker for design docs — creativity meets rigour. The Connector generates bold ideas while the Analytical Thinker stress-tests them.
  • Driver + Supporter for stakeholder work — decisiveness meets empathy. The Driver pushes for outcomes while the Supporter ensures relationships are maintained.
  • Connector + Supporter for team retrospectives — energy meets safety. The Connector keeps the session lively while the Supporter ensures everyone feels comfortable contributing.
  • When conflict arises between a Driver and a Supporter, mediate by slowing the Driver down and encouraging the Supporter to speak up. The friction is usually productive if you manage the pace.
💡

Map your team informally. Which styles are represented? Where are the gaps? You do not need to hire to fill gaps — you can also develop them. Help an Analytical Thinker practice presenting with energy. Help a Connector practice writing structured proposals. The team gets more balanced as each person grows.

Section 6

Adapting Your Leadership

The practical payoff of understanding styles is that you stop leading everyone the same way. You adapt your delegation, feedback, meeting style, and conflict approach to match what each person needs — not what feels natural to you.

Adapting by Situation

How to Adapt Across 5 Key Leadership Activities

Situation Analytical Thinker Driver Connector Supporter
Delegating Give written brief with details, set checkpoint Give goal and constraints, get out of their way Co-create vision, pair with someone structured Explain why it matters and who it helps, clear process
Giving feedback Specific, data-driven, written when possible Direct and brief, no hedging Lead with positive, be specific on what to change Safe 1:1 setting, gentle but honest
In meetings Give them the agenda in advance, do not put them on the spot Keep it short, drive to decisions Let them brainstorm, redirect when off-track Invite them explicitly, create safety for dissent
During conflict Ask for their analysis of the situation, give time to respond in writing Be direct about the issue, do not dance around it Acknowledge the emotional impact first Reassure that the relationship is safe, then address the issue
Celebrating wins Acknowledge the quality and thoroughness of their work specifically Keep it brief and genuine, do not overdo it Celebrate publicly with energy Personal, private recognition means more than public praise

How the Same Message Lands Differently

Consider the message: "We need to change our deployment process." Here is what each style wants to know:

Style What They Want to Know
Analytical Thinker What data supports this? What are the risks? Can I see the proposal in writing?
Driver What is the timeline? What do you need from me? Let us move.
Connector How does the team feel about this? Can we brainstorm improvements together?
Supporter Why is this changing? How will it affect the team? Will there be support during the transition?
⚠

These are tendencies, not boxes. Most people are a blend of two or more styles that shift depending on context. Use this as a lens for adapting your approach, not as a label for categorising people. The moment you say "oh, they are a Blue" and stop there, you have stopped seeing the whole person.

Treat others not as you would like to be treated, but as they need to be treated. That is the platinum rule of leadership.

📚

Go deeper: Read Inside-Out Leadership by Rajiv Vij for the inner work of understanding your leadership wiring. For team composition, The Five Dysfunctions of a Team by Patrick Lencioni shows how complementary styles prevent dysfunction.
