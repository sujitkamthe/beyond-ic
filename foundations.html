Section 1

The Leadership Mindshift

The transition from individual contributor to leader is not a promotion — it is a fundamental change in how you define success. Great leaders develop a new operating system for how they think, act, and measure their impact.

IC Thinking vs. Leader Thinking

The table below captures the core shift. Neither column is wrong — the left column is how strong ICs operate. The right column is how those same skills evolve when you begin leading others.

IC Thinking Leader Thinking
I succeed when I deliver results I succeed when my team delivers quality results
I fix things myself I build a culture where everyone improves
I review MRs for correctness I review MRs as teaching moments
I answer the Slack question I ensure the right person responds and builds context
I track my own progress I track the team's progress and surface risks early
I fill the dev huddle I create space for everyone to contribute
I avoid confrontation I have honest conversations because I care about growth

Four Growth Mindsets to Develop

The Enabler

Great leaders build systems and teach others rather than doing everything themselves. Your goal is to make yourself replaceable in day-to-day tasks so you can focus on what only you can do: setting direction, removing blockers, and growing people.

Ask yourself: "Am I doing this because only I can, or because it is faster if I do?"

The Courageous Communicator

Honest feedback delivered with empathy is one of the greatest gifts a leader can offer. Avoiding hard conversations does not protect people — it deprives them of the chance to grow. Great leaders learn to be direct and kind at the same time.

Ask yourself: "Is there feedback I have been holding back that could help someone improve?"

The Balanced Challenger

Being direct without being harsh is a learnable skill. The key is to critique the work, not the person. "This approach has a scalability risk" lands very differently than "You didn't think this through." Great leaders separate the idea from the individual.

Ask yourself: "Am I challenging the work, or am I challenging the person?"

The Proactive Leader

Tracking progress, surfacing risks, and asking hard questions early prevents small issues from becoming crises. Great leaders develop a habit of looking around corners — not to micromanage, but to clear the path before the team hits a wall.

Ask yourself: "What could go wrong in the next two weeks that I can address today?"

💡

The Litmus Test: "If I took a 2-week vacation, would the team's quality, practices, and delivery hold?" If the answer is no, your next priority is building the systems and habits that make it yes.

Think Like a Problem Solver

Most teams do not lack talent — they lack a systematic way to find and fix the real problems. Great leaders develop the discipline to look past symptoms and address root causes.

The Problem-Solving Framework

See → Analyse → Root Cause → Prioritise → Fix → Repeat

  1. See: Notice the symptom. Something feels off — delivery is slow, quality is dropping, morale is low.
  2. Analyse: Gather data. Talk to the team. Look at the evidence, not assumptions.
  3. Root Cause: Ask "why" until you reach a systemic cause, not a person to blame.
  4. Prioritise: Not all problems are equal. Focus on what will unlock the most progress.
  5. Fix: Implement the change. Make it small, concrete, and time-bound.
  6. Repeat: Problems are never "solved forever." Build a rhythm of continuous improvement.

The "5 Whys" Technique

The 5 Whys is a simple but powerful way to trace a symptom back to its systemic root cause. The key is to keep asking "why" and resist the urge to stop at a surface-level answer.

Example 1: Bugs Keep Hitting Production

  1. Why are bugs reaching production? → They are not caught during testing.
  2. Why are they not caught during testing? → Test coverage is low for critical paths.
  3. Why is test coverage low? → Developers skip writing tests to meet deadlines.
  4. Why do they skip tests to meet deadlines? → Estimates do not include time for testing.
  5. Why do estimates not include testing? → The team has no shared definition of "done" that includes test coverage.

Root cause: Missing team standard — no definition of "done" that includes test expectations. Fix the system, not the individuals.

Example 2: Tech Debt Keeps Piling Up

  1. Why is tech debt increasing? → The team never has time to address it.
  2. Why is there never time? → Every sprint is fully packed with feature work.
  3. Why is every sprint fully packed? → The team does not allocate capacity for tech debt.
  4. Why is capacity not allocated? → There is no agreed policy for balancing features and tech health.
  5. Why is there no agreed policy? → Nobody has made the case for it or proposed a structure.

Root cause: Missing process — no established norm for allocating capacity to technical health. The fix is structural, not motivational.

Symptoms vs. Root Causes

When you encounter a recurring problem, the visible symptom is rarely the real issue. Here are common examples of where teams get stuck:

Symptom (What You See) Root Cause (What's Actually Happening)
People write sloppy code No clear standards defined; no enforcement in reviews
Bugs keep reaching production No shared definition of "done" that includes testing
Developers skip writing tests Estimates don't account for test time; no team norm around testing
The same issues come up again and again No retrospective follow-through; fixes aren't tracked or enforced
Nobody speaks up in dev huddles Format doesn't create safety; same people always dominate
Knowledge lives in one person's head No pairing culture, no documentation norms, no rotation
Tech debt grows unchecked No agreed capacity allocation; no visibility to stakeholders
People seem unmotivated Lack of autonomy, unclear purpose, or no growth opportunities

Key Insight

Almost every root cause is a missing system — a missing process, norm, incentive, or skill. When you find yourself blaming individuals, you haven't gone deep enough. Great leaders keep asking "why" until they find the system to fix.

The Impact/Effort Prioritisation Matrix

Once you have identified problems and root causes, you need to decide where to invest your limited time. This classic 2x2 matrix helps you choose wisely:

High Impact, Low Effort

Do these first. Quick wins that create momentum and build confidence. Examples: defining a team "done" checklist, setting up a PR template, adding a 5-minute retro follow-up to standups.

High Impact, High Effort

Plan and invest. These are worth doing but need a roadmap. Examples: building a comprehensive testing strategy, restructuring the team's on-call rotation, establishing a mentoring programme.

Low Impact, Low Effort

Do if time permits. Nice-to-haves that won't move the needle much but cost little. Don't let these crowd out high-impact work. Examples: minor tooling tweaks, cosmetic process changes.

Low Impact, High Effort

Avoid. These consume disproportionate effort for minimal return. Examples: rewriting a working system "just because," gold-plating internal tools, over-engineering processes.

The Problem Statement Template

When you have identified a problem worth solving, write it down clearly. A well-structured problem statement aligns the team and prevents drift:

Problem Statement Template

  • Problem: What is happening? Describe the specific, observable issue.
  • Impact: Why does it matter? Quantify if possible (time lost, bugs shipped, velocity impact).
  • Root Cause: What systemic issue is driving this? (Use the 5 Whys to find it.)
  • Proposed Fix: What concrete change will address the root cause?
  • Owner: Who is responsible for driving this to completion?
  • Checkpoint: When will we review progress? (Set a specific date, not "sometime next month.")

6 Prioritisation Pitfalls

Even skilled leaders fall into these traps. Recognising them is the first step to avoiding them:

The Shiny Object Trap

Working on exciting new things instead of sprint commitments. It feels productive but often means the important work slips.

Ask yourself: "Is this what the team committed to this sprint, or am I chasing something new?"

The Comfortable Problem Trap

Gravitating toward technical problems while avoiding people or culture issues. Tech problems feel safe; people problems feel messy.

Ask yourself: "Am I solving the problem that matters most, or the one I am most comfortable with?"

The Everything is Urgent Trap

When everything is a priority, nothing is. This trap leads to constant firefighting and no strategic progress.

Ask yourself: "If I could only do one thing this week, what would unlock the most value?"

The Fix It Myself Trap

Jumping in to solve things personally instead of coaching others through it. Faster today, slower every day after.

Ask yourself: "If I solve this for them, what learning opportunity am I taking away?"

The Symptom Chasing Trap

Addressing surface-level symptoms instead of digging to root causes. The same problems return, often worse.

Ask yourself: "Have I asked 'why' enough times, or am I putting a plaster on this?"

The Invisible Progress Trap

Doing important work (culture building, mentoring, process improvement) that nobody sees or measures. Without visibility, it gets deprioritised.

Ask yourself: "Am I making my improvement work visible, or will it quietly get crowded out?"

The Daily Priority Check

Three Questions to Start Each Day

  1. What is the single most important thing I can do today? Not the most urgent — the most important.
  2. What is blocking my team right now? If someone is stuck, unblocking them multiplies your impact.
  3. What am I avoiding? The thing you keep pushing to tomorrow is often the thing that matters most.

Decision Making & Effective Moderation

Teams do not get stuck because they lack ideas. They get stuck because they cannot converge on a decision and move forward. Great leaders develop the skill of driving discussions to clear outcomes.

Why Discussions Stall

No Clear Goal

The discussion starts without a defined outcome. People share opinions, but nobody knows what they are deciding. A discussion without a decision question is just a conversation.

Waiting for Consensus

The group waits until everyone agrees, which may never happen. Consensus is ideal but not always possible. Leaders learn to recognise when alignment is "good enough" to move.

Nobody Drives to Conclusion

Ideas are shared, concerns are raised, but nobody synthesises them into a decision. Someone needs to say: "Here is what I am hearing. Here is what I propose. Any objections?"

Fear of Being Wrong

People hesitate to commit because they fear making the wrong call. But in most cases, a decent decision made quickly beats a perfect decision made too late.

One-Way Door vs. Two-Way Door Decisions

A Framework for Decision Speed

Not all decisions carry the same weight. Categorising decisions by reversibility helps you move faster where you can and be more careful where you must:

  • Two-Way Door (most decisions): Reversible. You can try it and change course if it does not work. Examples: choosing a library, trying a new sprint format, restructuring a standup. Decide quickly, learn, adjust.
  • One-Way Door (few decisions): Hard or impossible to reverse. Examples: choosing a primary database, committing to a public API contract, major architectural choices. Take time, gather input, document the reasoning.

Most decisions feel like one-way doors but are actually two-way doors. When in doubt, ask: "What would it cost to reverse this?" If the answer is "a few hours of work," move fast.

The Time-Box Rule

When a Discussion Goes Past 30 Minutes

  1. Name it: "We have been discussing this for a while. Let me try to summarise where we are."
  2. Summarise the options: Distil the discussion into 2–3 concrete options with trade-offs.
  3. Identify blockers: "What specific information are we missing that prevents a decision?"
  4. Propose a path: "I suggest we go with Option B. Here is why. Does anyone have a strong objection?"
  5. Set a deadline: If the decision truly cannot be made now, assign an owner and a deadline: "Let's collect X data by Thursday and decide then."

Disagree and Commit

Moving Forward Without Full Consensus

Not everyone will agree with every decision. "Disagree and commit" means:

  • Everyone gets a fair chance to voice their perspective
  • The decision-maker considers all input thoughtfully
  • Once a decision is made, the whole team commits fully — even those who disagreed
  • The team agrees to revisit the decision at a defined checkpoint if results warrant it

This is not about silencing dissent. It is about preventing endless debate from paralysing the team. Healthy teams can disagree openly, decide, and move forward together.

The Effective Moderation Playbook

7 Steps to Facilitate Discussions to Outcomes

  1. Set the frame: Start by stating the decision question, the time available, and what a good outcome looks like.
  2. Gather perspectives: Invite input from everyone — especially quiet voices. Ask directly: "What do you think?" rather than waiting for volunteers.
  3. Listen for themes: As people talk, mentally group ideas. Look for convergence and divergence.
  4. Name the tension: If there is disagreement, name it explicitly: "It sounds like we are torn between speed and thoroughness. Let's talk about that trade-off."
  5. Synthesise options: Restate the discussion as 2–3 clear options. "So our choices seem to be A, B, or C. Here are the trade-offs of each."
  6. Drive to decision: Propose a recommendation and check for objections. "I suggest Option B. Does anyone see a risk we have not discussed?"
  7. Confirm and document: State the decision, the rationale, and any follow-up actions. Send a summary so there is a written record.

Common Ambiguous Situations

Requirements are vague ▼

Resist the temptation to guess what stakeholders want. Instead: list what you know, what you don't know, and what assumptions you are making. Share that list with stakeholders and ask them to confirm or correct. Start building from the parts that are clear while you clarify the rest.

The key move: Transform vagueness into specific questions. "Should the report include historical data?" is answerable. "What do you want in the report?" is not.

Two people disagree on approach ▼

First, make sure both sides are arguing about the same thing. Often disagreements dissolve when you realise people are optimising for different goals. Make the criteria explicit: "Are we optimising for speed to market, long-term maintainability, or both?"

If the disagreement is genuine, ask each person to state the strongest version of the other person's argument (steelmanning). Then evaluate trade-offs together. If no resolution emerges, use the Two-Way Door test: "Can we try one approach and switch if it doesn't work?"

Critical bug but release is tomorrow ▼

Three questions to answer fast: (1) What is the actual blast radius if we release with this bug? (2) Is there a quick, safe mitigation (feature flag, config change, partial rollback)? (3) What is the cost of delaying the release by one day?

Often the answer is to release with the bug behind a feature flag, fix it in a follow-up, and do a post-mortem. The worst option is usually a last-minute "quick fix" under pressure — that is how you create a second bug.

Nobody has enough context ▼

This is a signal, not a blocker. Acknowledge the gap and decide how to fill it. Assign someone to gather the missing context with a specific deadline: "Can you spend 2 hours on this and share findings by end of day?"

In the meantime, identify what you can decide with current information. Often, 80% of the decision is clear even when 20% is uncertain. Make the 80% decision and mark the 20% as a known risk to revisit.

Discussion keeps going in circles ▼

If the same points are being repeated, the discussion needs a reset, not more time. Try: "I notice we are covering the same ground. Let me capture the two main positions on the whiteboard." Seeing the options written down often makes the trade-offs clearer.

If it is still circular after that, it usually means the group lacks either information or authority. Name which one: "I think we need X data before we can decide" or "I think we need Y person in the room to make this call."

💡

When in doubt, choose the option that is (1) reversible, (2) gives you more information, and (3) unblocks the team fastest.

Managing Your Time and Energy

The shift from IC to leader changes how you should spend your time. Many leaders fill their days with the same activities as before — coding, debugging, reviewing — and wonder why leadership work never gets done. Intentional time management is how you create space for the work that only a leader can do.

Key Insight

If you spend 100% of your time on the same work you did as an IC, you have 0% capacity for leadership. The transition requires deliberately reallocating your time — and that means doing less of what you are comfortable with.

How Leaders Should Spend Their Time

The 40/30/20/10 Guideline

This is not a rigid formula, but a useful mental model for how an engineering leader's week should roughly break down:

  • 40% — Enabling the team: MR reviews with teaching feedback, pairing sessions, unblocking people, answering questions, 1:1s. This is your highest-leverage work.
  • 30% — Strategic and improvement work: Setting standards, driving process improvements, tech vision, planning, stakeholder communication. This is the work that only you can do.
  • 20% — Hands-on technical work: Coding, debugging, spikes. You stay sharp technically but you are not the primary producer. Contribute to the most architecturally significant work, not routine stories.
  • 10% — Learning and growth: Reading, preparing sessions, staying current on the ecosystem, reflecting on what is working and what is not.

Why this matters: Most leaders default to 80% hands-on coding and 20% everything else. The team does not grow, quality does not improve, and the leader burns out wondering why nothing changes.

Protecting Time for Leadership Work

Block Focus Time

Protect 2 hours daily for proactive leadership work — MR reviews, 1:1 prep, standards documentation, stakeholder updates. Treat it like a meeting that cannot be moved. If this time does not exist on your calendar, it will not happen.

Batch Reactive Work

Slack messages, questions, and small requests are important but they fragment deep work. Designate 2-3 windows per day for responding to messages rather than staying in real-time mode all day. Communicate this to the team so they know when to expect responses.

Say No Strategically

Every "yes" to a meeting, a task, or a favour is a "no" to something else. Before accepting, ask: "Is this the highest-impact use of my time right now?" Learn to say: "I cannot take this on this sprint, but here is who can help."

Audit Your Week Monthly

At the end of each month, look at how you actually spent your time. How much was enabling? How much was strategic? How much was coding? If the ratio is wrong, adjust next month. What gets measured improves.

Common Time Traps for Leaders

"I will just fix this myself — it is faster" ▼

Why it is a trap: It is faster today, but it means you will be fixing similar things forever. Every time you fix something instead of teaching someone else to fix it, you make yourself more of a bottleneck.

Better: Pair with the person for 30 minutes. It takes longer now but saves you hundreds of hours over the next year.

"I need to be in every meeting" ▼

Why it is a trap: Being in every meeting means you have no time for the work that comes out of meetings. Presence is not the same as impact.

Better: For each recurring meeting, ask: "What would happen if I stopped attending?" If the answer is "nothing," stop attending. Delegate meeting attendance to team members who would grow from the exposure.

"I am too busy for 1:1s this week" ▼

Why it is a trap: 1:1s are not administrative overhead — they are your primary tool for understanding what is really happening on the team, giving feedback, and growing people. Skipping them consistently means you are flying blind.

Better: 1:1s are the last thing to cancel, not the first. Even a 15-minute check-in is better than nothing.

"I will do the leadership work after I finish my stories" ▼

Why it is a trap: Stories never "finish." There is always another one. If leadership work only happens when story work is done, it never happens.

Better: Commit to fewer stories. Make space for leadership work by taking on 60-70% of the story load you would take as an IC. The remaining 30-40% is your leadership capacity.

💡

The weekly reflection: Every Friday, spend 10 minutes asking yourself: "Did I spend my time this week on the things that only a leader can do? Or did I fill it with IC work because it felt more comfortable?" Honest self-awareness is the first step to better time allocation.
