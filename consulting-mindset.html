The Consulting Mindset

Leading engineering teams in a services context — where the goal is not just to ship, but to leave the client stronger than you found them.

Section 1

What Makes Consulting Leadership Different

If you lead a team in a consulting or services context, your job is subtly but fundamentally different from leading in a product company. You are temporarily embedded in someone else’s business, with their codebase, their stakeholders, their culture, and their constraints. Your team ships code — but your real product is the client’s long-term capability.

Key Insight

In a product company, success is measured by what you build and how long it lasts. In consulting, success is measured by what the client can build and sustain after you leave. Every decision — technical, process, people — must pass the test: “Will this still work six months after we are gone?” If the answer is no, you have not delivered value. You have created a dependency.

Product Engineering vs. Consulting Engineering

Dimension Product Company Consulting Engagement
Your time horizon Years — you will maintain what you build Months to a couple of years — someone else will maintain it
Your success metric Features shipped, systems running Client capability, trust, referenceability
Your users End customers Client end customers and the client engineering team
Your biggest risk Building the wrong thing Building the right thing in a way the client cannot maintain
Your team’s loyalty Aligned by default — same company, same goals Split — they belong to your firm but work for the client
Your hardest conversation “We need to deprecate this” “We recommend against what you have asked for”
Your measure of a good engagement The feature launched on time The client team could run it without us
💡

The consulting leader wears three hats at once: technical leader for their team, trusted advisor to the client, and coach for the client’s engineers. When any of these three gets neglected, the engagement weakens. The hardest part is knowing which hat the moment calls for.

Section 2

The Consulting Mindset: Build, Do Not Depend

The core mindset shift in consulting is this: your goal is to make yourself unnecessary. Not tomorrow — but deliberately, over time. A client who cannot function without you is not a success story. It is a liability — for them, because they have no resilience; and for you, because dependency rarely renews. The clients who come back and refer you are the ones whose teams you made stronger.

Key Insight

“Making yourself unnecessary” is not about leaving. It is about building so much capability in the client team that if you left tomorrow, the work would continue. Ironically, this is also the surest path to being asked back for the next problem. Clients re-engage the firms that made them stronger last time, not the ones that kept them dependent.

The Four Gifts of a Consulting Leader

In every engagement, aim to leave behind four things. These are the gifts that outlast your presence — and they compound the trust that wins you the next engagement.

The Four Gifts

  1. Capability

    The client team can do what you did. Not just maintain it — evolve it. This means pairing, teaching, and deliberately stepping back as they step forward. Capability is the gift that makes the next three possible.

  2. Clarity

    The client understands why things were built the way they were — the trade-offs, the alternatives considered, the assumptions behind the choices. When a new engineer joins six months from now and asks “why did we do it this way?”, the answer should be findable, not lost.

  3. Confidence

    The client team believes they can handle what comes next. Confidence comes from practice under your guidance — letting them lead incidents, drive releases, present to stakeholders while you still have their back. Confidence cannot be transferred; it has to be earned while you are still there to catch them.

  4. Continuity

    The systems, processes, and relationships you built are designed to outlast you. Documentation that is accurate. Runbooks that work when paged. Relationships between the client’s engineers and their business stakeholders that you have deliberately fostered rather than stood between.

Dependency vs. Capability

Common Pitfall: Building Dependency

You become the person the client calls for every critical decision. Your team writes the code, owns the deployments, gets paged for incidents. The client loves you. But the moment you disengage, their systems start to atrophy — because you were the system. This feels great for the engagement P&L in the short term. It is devastating for the client and eventually for the relationship.

Better Approach: Building Capability

Every major decision is a teaching moment for the client team. Every incident is an opportunity to coach their engineers through resolution. Every deployment pairs a client engineer with a consultant engineer. You measure your success not by how much the client relies on you, but by how confidently they operate what you built together. The client becomes stronger; the relationship becomes a partnership, not a lifeline.

⚠

Watch for the “hero trap.” When a client praises you as “the only one who understands our system,” take it as a warning, not a compliment. It means you have not transferred enough. A better outcome is when the client says, “Our team can explain this system as well as you can.”

Section 3

Client Communication

In a consulting engagement, communication with the client is not overhead — it is the work. The client is buying outcomes they cannot yet see, and much of what builds their confidence is how you talk about the work, not just the work itself. Predictable, proactive, honest communication is the single highest-leverage habit you can build.

The Three Levels of Client Stakeholder

Most engagements have three distinct stakeholder levels. Each needs different communication — frequency, depth, and framing. Confusing them is one of the most common causes of engagement friction.

The Sponsor

Usually the person who signed the contract — a VP, director, or head of engineering. Cares about business outcomes, risk, and ROI. Wants to hear: “Are we on track? What is worrying you? What decisions do you need from me?” Communicate monthly at minimum, with an executive summary. Never surprise this person.

The Product / Business Owner

The person who owns the what and why. Cares about scope, priorities, and what the business will actually see. Wants to hear: “Here is what we shipped, here is what is next, here is where we need your input.” Communicate weekly with a showcase or written update. This is your day-to-day collaboration partner.

The Engineering Counterpart

The tech lead, architect, or engineering manager on the client side. Cares about technical choices, code quality, operational fitness, and whether their team is growing. Wants to hear: “Here is how we made this decision, here is what we are teaching the team.” Communicate continuously — in standups, MR reviews, design discussions.

The No-Surprises Rule

What “No Surprises” Actually Means

A surprise is any piece of information the client learns later than you did — and wishes they had known sooner. The client is never upset that a risk exists. They are upset that they heard about it too late to do anything about it.

  1. If you see a risk on Monday, the client hears about it by Tuesday. Not in the Friday report. Not in the next showcase. Immediately.
  2. If a deadline is at risk, you say so the moment you know — not when it slips. Early warning gives the client options. Late warning takes options away.
  3. If a technical choice will surprise them later, flag it now. “We are choosing Postgres over DynamoDB because of X. If that is a concern, let us discuss before we invest further.”
  4. If something has gone wrong, tell them before they ask. The sentence “We found a bug that leaked into production last night — here is what happened, here is what we are doing” builds more trust than hiding the incident could ever preserve.

Why this matters: trust is built in the small moments where you could have hidden something and did not. The client remembers those. They are the reason they refer you.

The Weekly Cadence

A predictable communication cadence protects both sides. The client knows when to expect updates — and crucially, when not to chase you. Your team knows what rhythm to plan around.

A Reliable Weekly Cadence

  1. Weekly status note (written, 1 page).

    Delivered every Friday. Covers: what shipped this week, what is next, risks, decisions needed. Short is good — executives read short. Long notes get skimmed.

  2. Weekly showcase or walkthrough (live, 30 min).

    Not a demo — a showcase. The team presents working software, the client sees real progress, questions are answered live. The client’s product owner should leave with a clear sense of momentum.

  3. Fortnightly or monthly sponsor check-in (live, 30 min).

    Executive-level, outcome-focused. No live demos. One page of summary: trajectory, risks, decisions. Use this to flag anything that needs sponsor-level support.

  4. Continuous technical touchpoints.

    Standups, MR reviews, design discussions with the client’s engineering counterpart. These are the ground-truth signals that keep everything else aligned.

Scripts for Hard Client Conversations

Knowing the right tone matters more than the exact words. These scripts are starting points that show how to be direct without being harsh, and collaborative without being vague.

Saying “no” to a client request ▼

Setting: The client has asked for something you believe is wrong — wrong tech choice, wrong scope, wrong priority.

“I want to flag a concern before we commit to this. Based on what we know, I would recommend a different approach — here is why. I could be wrong; you know your business better than we do. But I would not be doing my job if I did not raise this. Can we spend 20 minutes walking through the trade-offs before we decide?”

Why this works: you lead with your job (to raise concerns), respect their authority (they decide), and ask for a conversation, not a reversal.

Flagging scope creep ▼

Setting: Small asks have accumulated, and the team is quietly over-committed.

“I want to share something I am noticing. Over the last three weeks, we have added these items to the scope (list). Each one made sense on its own. Collectively, they have pushed the original milestone out by about two weeks. I do not want to quietly absorb this and then surprise you later. Here are our options: reduce scope on the original plan, extend the timeline, or add capacity. Which of these works best for you?”

Why this works: you show evidence, avoid blame, and present choices rather than problems.

Delivering bad news (a missed deadline, a production issue) ▼

Setting: Something has gone wrong and the client will be affected.

“I wanted to get ahead of this and tell you directly. We hit an issue last night — here is what happened in plain terms. Here is what we know, what we do not yet know, and what we are doing about it right now. I will send you a written update by 4pm today, and again tomorrow morning. If you have questions in the meantime, call me directly.”

Why this works: plain language, no defensiveness, a clear next step, and an open channel. Clients remember how you handled problems long after they forget the problems themselves.

Managing expectations when a deadline is tight ▼

Setting: The client is anchored on a date that the team will struggle to hit.

“I want to be straight with you about the date. To hit it, we would need to cut either X or Y. Without one of those, we are looking at a week of slippage, and we would be shipping something less robust than we would recommend. What matters most to you — the date, the scope, or the quality? Tell me, and we will design the plan around that.”

Why this works: you are not refusing the date — you are making the trade-offs visible. Clients almost always have a clear priority once forced to choose.

Pushing back on a technical choice the client is attached to ▼

Setting: A client engineer insists on a technology or pattern you think is the wrong fit.

“Help me understand what drew you to this choice — I want to make sure I am not missing context. Here is how we have been thinking about it, and here is what worries me about that path. I am not trying to override you; I just want us both to be looking at the same trade-offs. If after that you still prefer this approach, we will make it work — but I want us to walk in with eyes open.”

Why this works: you invite their reasoning first, share your concern second, and acknowledge their authority. This builds respect even if they still choose the path you disagreed with.

💡

The “one-page status” pattern: every week, produce a single page the client can forward to their executives without edits. Three sections: What shipped (outcomes, not activity), What is next (with confidence), Risks and decisions needed (honest, specific). If your weekly note is longer than a page, you are hiding the signal in the noise.

Section 4

Knowledge Transfer as a First-Class Practice

In most engagements, knowledge transfer is treated as an end-of-engagement activity — a rushed month of documentation and demos before the consultants roll off. By that point, it is too late. KT done in the final month is always too little. KT woven into daily practice from day one is always enough.

Key Insight

Knowledge transfer is not a phase. It is the way you work. If your team is writing code the client engineers have not seen, if decisions are made in your internal channels without client engineers in the room, if context lives in your heads and not in shared artefacts — then KT is already failing, and adding a “KT month” at the end will not fix it. The discipline is to build KT into every day, from day one.

The Four Modes of Knowledge Transfer

Different knowledge moves through different channels. Documentation alone does not transfer understanding. Pairing alone does not scale. A mature KT practice uses all four modes, each for what it is best at.

1. Written Artefacts

Best for: reference material, onboarding, compliance, decisions. Architecture Decision Records, runbooks, system diagrams, README files that actually stay current. Written artefacts must be maintained, not just created — stale documentation is worse than none, because it erodes trust in every document.

Quality bar: a new engineer joining in six months can use this without asking anyone.

2. Pairing and Mobbing

Best for: transferring judgment, not just facts. How you debug, how you decide between two patterns, how you read a stack trace. These skills cannot be written down — they transfer by watching and doing. Pair client engineers with consultant engineers on real work, not training exercises.

Quality bar: the client engineer can drive the next similar task alone.

3. Teaching Sessions

Best for: concepts, patterns, frameworks. Short, focused sessions — 45 minutes with a small group. The client engineers learn the why, not just the what. Even better: have the client team teach back what they learned the following week. If they can teach it, they own it.

Quality bar: a client engineer can explain the concept to a new joiner without referring to notes.

4. Recorded Walkthroughs

Best for: one-time explanations that many people will need later. A 10-minute screen recording walking through a subsystem. An architect narrating a trade-off decision. A runbook demo. Asynchronous, reusable, and captures tone and context that written docs lose.

Quality bar: a client engineer who joins two months from now can watch this and get unstuck without asking.

The KT Maturity Ladder

Knowledge transfer is not binary. It progresses through stages. Understanding where you are on this ladder helps you know what to invest in next.

Five Stages of KT Maturity

  1. Stage 1: Individual knowledge.

    One consultant knows how a piece of the system works. Nobody else — on either side — does. This is where every engagement starts and every risky engagement stays.

  2. Stage 2: Paired knowledge.

    Two consultants know it. The bus factor is two, but the client still depends entirely on your firm.

  3. Stage 3: Team knowledge.

    Your whole team understands it. Internally robust, but the client cannot yet operate it alone.

  4. Stage 4: Client team knowledge.

    A client engineer can make changes, debug issues, and onboard a new joiner. This is the minimum bar for a healthy engagement.

  5. Stage 5: Self-sustaining client knowledge.

    The client team can evolve the system independently: add new features, adopt new patterns, make architectural decisions. They still consult you — but as peers, not as dependents. This is the bar to aim for.

Why this matters: if your whole engagement is at Stage 3 in month 10 of 12, you have two months to get to Stage 4. That is not enough time. Plan for each major area to reach Stage 4 well before the final quarter.

KT as Daily Practice, Not a Final Phase

Common Pitfall: KT as a Handover Activity

“We will do KT in the last month.” The team spends eleven months building in consultant-only channels, writing code client engineers never see, making decisions client engineers were never part of. Then in month twelve, they frantically write documents and run sessions that dump information the client cannot absorb in time. The client is frustrated, the team is exhausted, and the knowledge does not transfer.

Better Approach: KT as Operating Model

Every MR has at least one client reviewer. Every design discussion includes a client engineer. Every incident is co-investigated. Every pairing session is cross-firm. Documentation is written as work happens, not after. The final month is a confirmation that KT already happened — not a scramble to do it for the first time.

Section 5

Team Ramp-Up on New Engagements

A new engagement starts the way a new leadership role starts — by listening before you change anything. But in consulting, you have less time, more stakeholders to align, and a team looking to you to absorb context quickly and translate it for them. The first two weeks set the trajectory for the next twelve months.

Four Landscapes to Map in the First Two Weeks

Before you can lead, you need to understand the terrain. Every client is different, and assumptions from a previous engagement can mislead you badly. Map these four landscapes deliberately.

The Business Landscape

How does the client actually make money? Who are their customers? What is changing in their market? Why are they hiring consultants right now — what is the underlying business pressure? This context informs every technical trade-off. A fintech under regulatory scrutiny needs different choices than a startup chasing growth.

The Technical Landscape

What is the existing stack? What is its real health (not what the docs say)? What is legacy, what is aspirational, what is load-bearing? Where are the bodies buried? Read the incident history, the recent MRs, the deployment pipeline. Data reveals what meetings hide.

The People Landscape

Who are the client engineers? What are their skill levels, aspirations, and frustrations? Who are the quiet experts? Who are the blockers? Who has informal influence? In consulting, you cannot hire or fire — you work with the team as-is. Understanding them is the highest-leverage investment of your first two weeks.

The Political Landscape

Who holds real decision-making power, versus who holds the title? What are the internal tensions — engineering vs. product, centralised vs. decentralised, team A vs. team B? Where is our engagement controversial, and with whom? Ignoring politics does not make it go away; it just means you will collide with it unprepared.

Seven Questions for Your First Sponsor Meeting

Questions That Reveal What Matters

  1. “What does success for this engagement look like, in your words, six months from now?”

    Reveals the real goal, which is often different from what is written in the statement of work.

  2. “What happens in your business if we succeed? What happens if we do not?”

    Reveals the stakes, and therefore where you need to be most careful.

  3. “Who else in your organisation cares whether this engagement succeeds? Who might be sceptical?”

    Reveals the political landscape you will need to navigate.

  4. “What has been tried before in this area, and why did it not work?”

    Reveals constraints you would otherwise walk into blind. Also signals respect for the organisation’s history.

  5. “How do you like to receive updates — format, frequency, what to include?”

    Saves months of friction. Some sponsors want one page a month; others want a weekly call. Ask, do not guess.

  6. “What should our team know about working with your organisation that nobody will tell us directly?”

    Invites the unsaid. Not every sponsor will answer, but the ones who do give you gold.

  7. “When we succeed, what would you like us to have left behind?”

    Anchors the engagement on capability, not just delivery. Use the answer to shape the KT plan from day one.

Onboarding Your Team to a New Client

Your team is looking to you to translate the client into something they can engage with. A good onboarding in the first week saves weeks of confusion in the following months.

A Team Onboarding Playbook

  1. Day 1: The business primer.

    Before a single line of code is written, spend 60 minutes walking the team through the client’s business. Who they are, how they make money, why we are here. Technical context without business context produces bad trade-offs.

  2. Days 2–3: The system primer.

    Walk through the architecture, the current pain points, the recent incidents. Include a client engineer in this session if possible — it builds relationships early and catches our misreadings.

  3. Week 1: Shadow and pair.

    Every team member pairs with a client engineer on real work within the first five days. Not a tutorial — a real task. This is the fastest way to build both context and relationships.

  4. Week 2: Stakeholder introductions.

    Introduce each team member to the client people they will work with — not just the sponsor, but the product owner, the engineering counterparts, the QA team. The team cannot build relationships if they have not been introduced.

  5. Weekly: The “what surprised you” retro.

    For the first four weeks, end each week with a 15-minute session asking: “What surprised you about this client this week?” These surprises are data. They reveal assumptions we brought that do not apply here.

⚠

Resist the urge to recommend in week one. The team will see things they think are obviously wrong — a broken process, a poor tech choice, a dysfunctional meeting. Before you recommend changes, ask: “What don’t I know yet about why this is the way it is?” The answer is almost always surprising. Confident early recommendations that turn out wrong are expensive — they cost trust you have not yet built.

Section 6

Technical Decisions in a Consulting Context

Every technical decision you make on a client’s system carries a cost — but the cost will be paid by them, not you. This changes the calculus profoundly. In a product company, “we will learn the new framework together” is fine. In a consulting context, it can leave the client with a system they cannot staff, maintain, or evolve after you leave.

Key Insight

The best technical choice in a consulting engagement is often not the most elegant, most modern, or most exciting one. It is the one the client can run six months after you leave. “Pragmatic” is not a compromise — it is the professional standard.

The Three Questions Before Any Major Technical Choice

The Consulting Technical Decision Filter

  1. Can the client hire for this?

    Is there a healthy talent pool for this language, framework, or tool in their market? Choosing something exotic may feel innovative, but it can leave the client unable to staff the team once you are gone. Boring, well-staffed choices are often the most respectful.

  2. Can the client team maintain this after we leave?

    Does the client team have (or can they realistically acquire in the engagement window) the skills to operate and evolve this? If it takes us a week to grasp it, it will take them months. That cost is real and belongs in the decision.

  3. Is the added complexity justified by the client’s actual needs — not ours?

    If we are introducing Kafka where a queue would do, or Kubernetes where a VM would do, the question is not “can we justify it?” but “is the client’s scale or problem actually calling for it?” Complexity is a cost they will pay for years. Never introduce it for our convenience.

The six-months-after test: imagine you have left. A client engineer two months into the role picks up a ticket that touches this choice. Will they be productive, or will they be stuck? If the honest answer is “stuck,” the choice is wrong regardless of its technical merits.

Tech Flex vs. Pragmatic Defaults

Common Pitfall: Tech Flexing

The consulting team introduces the latest, most sophisticated stack — event sourcing, a service mesh, a bespoke DSL — because they have the skills and the client is paying. It ships. It is elegant. It makes the case study glow. Twelve months later the client is paying a firm (often a different one) to untangle it, because nobody on their team can evolve it safely.

Better Approach: Pragmatic Defaults

Choose boring technology the client can hire for, operate, and evolve. Introduce novel approaches only where the business problem genuinely demands them — and only with the client’s informed buy-in. When you introduce something new, invest heavily in teaching, documenting, and building the client team’s confidence with it. Leave them stronger, not more dependent on specialists.

Making Trade-offs Visible

In a consulting context, the worst outcome is not that you choose wrong — it is that the client did not know there was a choice to make. Every significant technical decision should be traceable: what did we consider, what did we choose, and why?

The ADR Discipline in Consulting

  1. Write ADRs for any decision that will outlast the engagement.

    Stack choices, architecture patterns, data model decisions, integration approaches. The test: “Will someone six months from now ask why we chose this?” If yes, write it down.

  2. Include the alternatives and why you rejected them.

    Future engineers will want to revisit the choice. Give them the reasoning, not just the outcome. A one-line answer in the ADR prevents hours of archaeology later.

  3. Co-author with a client engineer.

    If a client engineer writes the ADR with you, you know the reasoning transferred. You also build a habit: ADRs become the client team’s artefact, not yours.

  4. Surface consequential ADRs to the sponsor.

    Not every ADR needs executive visibility. But the ones with cost, risk, or strategic implications do. “We chose X over Y because Z — wanted to flag before we invest further” is a short message that prevents large surprises.

💡

The leave-behind review: once a quarter, walk through the system with your engineering counterpart and ask, “If we left next month, what are the three areas you would be least confident about?” Their answers are your KT priorities. This single practice, done quarterly, transforms how ready the client is to take over.

Section 7

Building Trust: The No-Surprises Principle

Trust is the currency of consulting. It is what lets you push back on a bad decision, ask for more time, raise an uncomfortable truth. And trust is not built in the big moments — it is built in the hundreds of small moments where you could have bent the truth and did not. The no-surprises principle is not a communication tactic. It is a trust-building operating model.

The Trust Ledger

Think of trust as a ledger with the client. Every interaction adds a credit or a debit. Credits compound — a client who trusts you gives you more latitude, more honesty, more openness. Debits also compound — once trust is damaged, every future interaction is more expensive.

Actions That Add Credits

  • You flag a risk before the client sees it
  • You recommend against work we would have been paid for, because it is not in the client’s interest
  • You admit a mistake before the client discovers it
  • You deliver exactly what you said, on the date you said
  • You give credit to the client team publicly
  • You push back respectfully when you disagree — and are right often enough that pushback is welcomed
  • You respond to the client’s anxiety with calm specifics, not reassurance

Actions That Add Debits

  • A deadline slips and the client finds out on the deadline
  • A bug reaches production and the client hears about it from their users
  • A technical choice surprises the client months later
  • A status report makes things sound better than they are
  • A claim is made that is later quietly walked back
  • The team takes credit for work a client engineer led
  • Difficult conversations are avoided until they become crises

Five Ways to Earn Trust Quickly

High-Leverage Trust Builders

  1. Deliver one small thing perfectly in the first two weeks.

    Not a grand plan — a small, visible, well-executed thing. A fixed flaky test, a clean dashboard, a CI improvement. This tells the client “these people do what they say.”

  2. Be early with bad news.

    The first time you raise a risk before the client noticed it, trust jumps. The second time, it compounds. This is the highest-leverage habit in consulting.

  3. Recommend against a request you would have been paid for.

    When you advise against something that would have added scope — because it is not in the client’s interest — trust accelerates. It tells them you are genuinely on their side.

  4. Credit the client team publicly.

    When a client engineer contributes to a win, name them in your showcase or status report. Not ours — theirs. This signal reaches their management and builds trust at multiple levels simultaneously.

  5. Show up under stress with calm specifics, not reassurance.

    When things go wrong, “it will be fine” is worthless. “Here is what happened, here is what we are doing in the next hour, here is when I will update you next” is priceless. Clients remember how you showed up in the hard moments.

Scripts for Trust-Building Moments

Admitting a mistake ▼

Setting: Your team caused the problem.

“I want to own this directly. We made the wrong call on X, and here is what it cost. Here is what we are doing to fix it, and here is what we have changed in how we work so it does not happen again. I am sorry. I am not asking you to forget this — I am asking you to hold us accountable for what we do next.”

Why this works: no deflection, no hedging, a concrete remediation, and a standing invitation to be held accountable. This is one of the most trust-positive moves you can make.

Recommending against paid work ▼

Setting: The client has asked you to build something you do not believe will deliver value.

“I want to flag something before we commit. We can absolutely build this — but I do not think it will deliver what you are hoping for, and I would not feel good taking on work that I expect to disappoint. Here is what I think would actually solve the underlying problem, and why I would recommend it instead. Happy to be wrong; you know things I do not. But I wanted to raise it before we start.”

Why this works: it costs you revenue in the short term and earns you a decade of trust. Clients remember the consultants who told them the truth — and they come back to them.

Proactively surfacing a risk ▼

Setting: You noticed something that could become a problem. The client has not seen it yet.

“I wanted to flag something proactively. It is not a problem today, but it has the potential to become one if we do not address it — here is what I am seeing, here is why it concerns me, and here are a couple of options for how to get ahead of it. I am not raising alarm; I just want it on your radar before it becomes urgent.”

Why this works: it communicates calm vigilance. Clients learn they will hear about problems from you before they hear about them from their users or their board.

Giving credit to the client team ▼

Setting: In a showcase or report, a client engineer contributed significantly to a win.

“I want to call out that this outcome was largely driven by [client engineer name]. They led the design, they pushed back on two of our assumptions that turned out to be wrong, and the final result is better for it. Our team contributed, but this was their win. I wanted to make sure that is visible.”

Why this works: it signals to the client’s management that we are growing their team, not just doing work on top of them. It builds the client engineer’s standing, which buys enormous goodwill with their leadership.

💡

Trust is asymmetric: it is built slowly and lost quickly. One avoided hard conversation, one hidden risk, one missed deadline without warning can undo months of good work. This is not a reason to be anxious — it is a reason to be disciplined. The habits in this section, practised consistently, are how consulting leaders earn the kind of trust that outlasts any single engagement.

Section 8

Common Traps in Consulting Leadership

These are the patterns that quietly erode consulting engagements. They rarely look like failures in the moment — many of them feel like strengths. Recognising them early lets you catch yourself before they compound.

Becoming the Indispensable Hero

The client calls you for every critical decision. You love being needed. The engagement feels strong. But you are building dependency, not capability — and the client will eventually resent the very dependence you created.

Ask yourself: “If I disappeared tomorrow, what would break? What can I transfer this week to change that answer?”

Saying Yes to Everything

You want to be seen as flexible and accommodating. So you absorb scope creep, shift priorities weekly, take on requests that should have been pushed back on. The team over-commits, quality drops, and eventually something misses — and by then, the client is surprised because you never said no.

Ask yourself: “Am I agreeing to this because it is right, or because it is uncomfortable to push back?”

Treating KT as Optional

The team is busy delivering; KT feels like a distraction. You tell yourself you will “do it properly at the end.” You will not. Every week of delayed KT is a week of knowledge locked in consultant heads — knowledge that becomes exponentially harder to transfer the longer it sits.

Ask yourself: “If we exited this engagement in 30 days, could the client operate what we built? What specifically would they struggle with?”

Hiding Problems to Protect the Relationship

You soften bad news, omit risks, or frame status reports more optimistically than reality warrants. You tell yourself you are protecting the relationship. You are protecting your comfort. The truth always catches up — and when it does, the cost to trust is much larger than the honest conversation you avoided.

Ask yourself: “Am I writing this update, or am I writing the update the client wishes I had written?”

Over-Engineering Because You Can

You are surrounded by strong engineers. The client is paying for quality. So you introduce patterns, abstractions, and infrastructure well beyond what the problem calls for. The result feels elegant but leaves the client with a system they cannot staff or evolve.

Ask yourself: “Would I make the same choice if my team had to staff and run this for the next three years?”

Under-Investing in the Client Team

The client engineers are slower, less experienced, less motivated than your team. It is faster to just do the work yourselves. But the client team is who you are here to grow. Every task your team does alone is a teaching moment missed. Every “we’ll handle it” is a small debit on the engagement’s real value.

Ask yourself: “In the last week, which tasks could a client engineer have led with our support? Why did we not set it up that way?”

Treating the Client as Adversary

The client pushes back, changes requirements, asks uncomfortable questions. It starts to feel like a contest. You and your team huddle, strategise how to “handle” the client. Once this framing takes hold, trust corrodes on both sides — and the team’s energy flows into managing the client instead of serving them.

Ask yourself: “Am I framing this client as a partner I am trying to help, or as an opposition I am trying to manage?”

Leaving Abruptly

The contract ends. The team rolls off. Nobody is designated to answer questions for the next month. The client is left with half-written docs and a team still figuring out what they do not know. This is how good engagements end badly — and end the referral chain with them.

Ask yourself: “Have we planned the last 60 days of this engagement with the same care as the first 60? Who is on-call for post-rolloff questions, and for how long?”

Measuring Success by Revenue, Not Outcomes

When decisions about staffing, scope, or renewal are driven primarily by internal revenue targets rather than the client’s actual needs, you start making choices a trusted advisor would not make. The client often senses it long before it is named. Protect the discipline of asking, “Is this the right thing for the client?” first, always.

Ask yourself: “Am I advocating for this because it is the right outcome for the client, or because it is the right outcome for our engagement P&L?”

Neglecting the Unglamorous Handover Work

The runbooks that never got updated. The CI alarms still pointing to your team’s Slack. The service account owned by a consultant who has rolled off. These small, unglamorous items accumulate and become the client’s problems the moment you leave. Audit them monthly — not in the final week.

Ask yourself: “What operational loose ends have I been meaning to tidy up? What happens to them if I do not tidy them this month?”
